Part II · Chapter 1
The Vision — A Planetary University

A university without a physical place is a YouTube channel. A university with one place is a campus. A university with fifty places — wired into a single institution, governed as one, credentialed as one, financed as one — is something the world has not yet built.

Artemis is designed to be that institution. It is not a network of affiliates, not a federation of independent schools, and not a franchise. It is one university, with one curriculum, one faculty, one degree, and one balance sheet — distributed across fifty colleges on six continents, anchored by three Central Nodes in Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore. A student who begins a degree in Kigali can complete a tutorial in Tokyo and defend a thesis in Venice, all within the same academic home. The campus is the planet.

The conviction behind this design is simple. The great problems of the twenty-first century — climate, governance, intelligence, biosecurity, migration, the legitimacy of institutions — are not national problems that occasionally spill across borders. They are planetary problems that happen to be administered nationally. A university built to address them cannot live in a single valley, however distinguished. It must be co-extensive with the territory of its questions.

The Artemis network at full build: 50 colleges on six continents, 15,000 students per Central Node, a planetary institution governed as one.

Artemis is also designed against a particular failure mode of contemporary higher education. The dominant model — a single campus, a four-year residential cohort, a tuition that prices out ninety percent of the world's qualified students — was invented in the nineteenth century and has barely been revised. It produces extraordinary outcomes for the small fraction who can access it, and it produces nothing for everyone else. We do not believe the answer is to scale that model by a factor of a thousand. We believe the answer is to redesign the model so that scale and quality compound rather than trade off.

The redesign rests on three commitments. First, tuition is set at three thousand dollars per year — a figure accessible to ninety percent of qualified students worldwide, and sufficient (when multiplied across sixty thousand students) to cover two thousand faculty in full. Second, the curriculum is unified across every college, taught by a single faculty, and certified by one degree. Third, the institution is owned, not leased; buildings are acquired outright, endowments are endowed, and the balance sheet is engineered so that the university becomes self-sustaining within twelve months of opening.

The Three Commitments

1. Access. $3,000/yr tuition — within reach of 90% of qualified students globally. 2. Unity. One curriculum, one faculty, one degree across all 50 colleges. 3. Permanence. Buildings owned, endowments endowed, self-sustaining within 12 months of launch.

The remainder of this prospectus explains how those three commitments become a concrete institution. It is, in effect, an engineering document. Every number is real, every line item is budgeted, every assumption is disclosed. The vision is the easy part; the rest of these pages are the hard part, and they are the reason we believe Artemis can be built.

Part II · Chapter 2
The Artemis Model

Three academic pillars, four curriculum commitments, fifty colleges, three Central Nodes, one degree. This is the architecture of the institution your founding gift will build.

The academic architecture of Artemis is built on four curriculum pillars that every student, in every college, completes regardless of discipline. Epistemology — how we know what we know — is taught not as a philosophy elective but as the substrate of every field, from computer science to architecture. Computational thinking is the algorithmic decomposition of problems across every domain, from literary analysis to epidemiology. Global systems are studied as integrated wholes: climate, finance, migration, supply chains, governance. And creative expression is required of every graduate, because we believe that making is knowing, and that an engineer who cannot write and a poet who cannot reason are equally unfinished.

These four pillars are delivered through nine degree programs in launch year — Computer Science, International Business, Environmental Science, Philosophy Politics & Economics, Biotechnology, Architecture & Urban Design, Data Science & Statistics, Global History, and Cognitive Science. Each is a four-year program taught through the Oxford-style tutorial method, with one faculty member meeting two to four students weekly for sustained Socratic engagement. The tutorial is not a supplement to lectures; it is the principal mode of instruction. Lectures are recorded and consumed asynchronously; the contact hour is the tutorial.

The tutorial model: small-cohort, faculty-led, asynchronous lectures. The same curriculum delivered in every college, certified by one degree.

The physical architecture is a network of fifty colleges organised into three tiers. The three Central Nodes — Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore — each host approximately five thousand students and serve as the convening, governance, and ceremonial seats of the university. Eight Tier A colleges (London, Tokyo, Berlin, Paris, Toronto, Sydney, Seoul, New York) host approximately twenty thousand students across the world's great knowledge capitals. Sixteen Tier B colleges (Nairobi, Mumbai, São Paulo, Cairo, Istanbul, Buenos Aires, Jakarta, Mexico City, and others) anchor major global cities. Twenty-three Tier C colleges provide regional access in cities like Kigali, Dhaka, Kampala, Karachi, Tashkent, and Quito.

The research architecture comprises nineteen Centers of Inquiry, each a permanently endowed, independently operating research center distributed across the network. Centers focus on synthetic intelligence, bio-regenerative arts, cosmological humanities, urban futures, biotech and life sciences, fintech and decentralized economics, health and bioethics, and twelve further domains. Forty-two active projects run across the centers in launch year, each with named investigators, funded fellowships, and a publication commitment under Artemis's seven-year release policy: research produced under the university's banner becomes open knowledge seven years after first publication.

The Network at Full Build

3 Central Nodes · 8 Tier A colleges · 16 Tier B colleges · 23 Tier C colleges = 50 colleges, 60,000 students, 2,000 faculty, 19 Centers of Inquiry, 9 degree programs, 1 unified degree.

The governance architecture is the least visible but the most consequential. Artemis is incorporated as a Delaware non-stock 501(c)(3) public charity in the United States, with parallel charitable entities in England & Wales (Charitable Incorporated Organisation) and Switzerland (Fondation under the Swiss Civil Code). No single individual controls more than one-third of board seats; a majority of independent directors is required at all times; an annual independent audit by a Big Four firm is published without exception. The institution is engineered, from day one, to outlive every person named in this document.

Part III · Chapter 3

How one hundred million dollars in founding capital becomes a three-hundred-million-dollar annual operating university, a $543M endowment, and thirteen thousand scholarships per year — within a decade.

The single most important number in this prospectus is not the $100M campaign goal. It is the relationship between that goal and what the institution produces once it is built. Founding capital is a one-time input. Annual revenue is a permanent output. The financial model is engineered so that the ratio between input and output is dramatically asymmetric in the donor's favor: one hundred million dollars in, three hundred million dollars per year out, indefinitely.

Why This Matters to a Founding Donor

Your gift is not consumed. It is converted — into a building, a professorship, a scholarship, a Center — that generates returns in perpetuity. Part III · Chapter 5
The Three Central Nodes

Three cities anchor the network. Each hosts five thousand students. Each bears a founding donor's name in perpetuity. Together they form the governance, innovation, and convening triangle of the planetary university.

The Central Nodes are not administrative capitals in the conventional sense. They are the three places where the university convenes itself — where the founding document is archived, where convocation is held, where the Board of Visitors meets, where the president resides. Each Node hosts approximately five thousand students and is the largest single institution in its host city. Each is named by a founding donor at the $25M level.

Venice Central Node
The Founding Seat · ~5,000 students
The founding seat — governance, convocation, the founding document archive. Canals and Renaissance palazzi repurposed as the academic heart. Venice was chosen because it is the city where East meets West, where the Renaissance was financed, and where a planetary institution can convene without belonging to any single nation.
San Francisco Central Node
Technology & Innovation Seat · ~5,000 students
The technology and innovation seat — proximity to Silicon Valley, the Forge incubator, biotech and computational research. The San Francisco Node is where the institution's research portfolio meets the capital markets that can scale it, and where the 5% innovation equity carry that funds the operating budget is generated.
Singapore Central Node
Asia-Pacific Seat · ~5,000 students
The Asia-Pacific seat — a neutral convening point for the Eastern hemisphere, fintech and urban futures research. Singapore was chosen for its neutrality, its regulatory sophistication, and its position as the natural meeting point of South Asia, East Asia, and Oceania. A founding donor may name this Node for $25M.
A Note on Selection

These three cities were chosen after a two-year review of more than forty candidate locations. Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore were selected because together they span every major timezone, every major legal tradition, and every major linguistic family — and because each is, in its own way, a city that has been rebuilt by trade and ideas. A planetary university must be rooted in places that understand planetary traffic.

Part IV · Chapter 6
Naming Opportunities

Every gift at every level carries a name. From a $12K student scholarship to a $25M Central Node, the naming architecture is designed so that no founding contribution is invisible — and so that every named asset is permanent.

Artemis's naming philosophy is simple. A named gift is not a plaque — it is an endowment. When you name a College, the College owns the building; your name is attached to that building for as long as the institution exists. When you name a Professorship, your gift is invested at 4.5% yield, and the income funds one faculty position in perpetuity. When you name a Scholarship, your gift covers one student's four-year education, and your name is recorded permanently in the founding Donor Roll. There is no temporary naming, no five-year term, no expiry.

The naming architecture is engineered for leverage. Consider the College naming math. A $5M Tier B College naming covers a property that, in most major global cities, would cost $8–12M to acquire and repurpose. The donor's $5M is matched against the building's acquisition; the campaign covers the remainder. The donor's name is attached to a permanent physical asset that houses two thousand students per year. Over a fifty-year building life, that is one hundred thousand students educated inside a building bearing the donor's name. The cost per student is fifty dollars.

The Distinguished Professorship math is even cleaner. A $5M endowment at 4.5% yield produces $225,000 per year, every year, indefinitely. A career-level faculty compensation package is approximately $200,000–$225,000 fully loaded. The endowment therefore funds one professor forever — and as the endowment compounds, the surplus accumulates, eventually funding a second position from the same gift. A founding donor who names five professorships creates a permanent academic department that will exist for as long as the university does.

The Naming Principle

No temporary naming. No five-year terms. No expiry. A named gift at Artemis is a permanent transfer of capital into a permanent asset of the institution. The donor's name is recorded in the founding charter, engraved at the named site, and protected by the same governance and audit regime that protects every other asset of the university.

Part IV · Chapter 8
How Founding Campaigns Work

Founding campaigns do not raise money from crowds. They raise money from a small number of people who understand that institutions are built before they are announced. The Artemis campaign follows the standard distribution: 70% from 10 donors, 20% from 20 donors, 10% from hundreds.

This distribution is not a preference — it is a structural fact about how institutions are founded. Yale's "For Humanity" campaign secured $3.5 billion in its quiet phase, before any public announcement, from fewer than fifty donors. Stanford's campaign raised more than 70% of its goal from lead donors before the public launch. The pattern is consistent because the logic is consistent: lead donors commit privately because they understand that their commitment is what makes the public campaign credible. Without the lead gifts, there is nothing to announce.

Ten lead donors at an average of $7M each — typically distributed across a Central Node naming, a College naming, and a Professorship or Center — secure seventy percent of the campaign. Part V · Chapter 10
Governance, Accountability & Tax

Three parallel legal entities across three jurisdictions. A board majority of independent directors. A Big Four audit, published annually. Tax deductibility in twelve major donor countries. The institutional architecture is engineered for permanence and transparency.

Legal Architecture
Three Entities, One Institution

Artemis is incorporated as three parallel legal entities, each tailored to the regulatory and tax requirements of its host jurisdiction. The primary vehicle is a Delaware non-stock 501(c)(3) public charity, which provides tax-deductible giving in the world's largest philanthropy market — a $499B annual market in 2024. The secondary vehicle, in England & Wales, is a Charitable Incorporated Organisation eligible for Gift Aid. The tertiary vehicle, planned for Switzerland, is a Fondation under the Swiss Civil Code, providing endowment management in a neutral jurisdiction.

The Ask

We are asking for one hundred million dollars from approximately thirty donors to build a university that will, within a decade, generate a five-hundred-and-forty-three-million-dollar endowment and educate sixty thousand students per year. We are asking you to be one of those thirty.

The ask is precise. The campaign requires ten lead gifts at an average of $7M each, and twenty major gifts at an average of $1M each. Every lead gift is structured around a named asset — a Central Node, a College, a Center of Inquiry, or a Distinguished Professorship — that becomes a permanent part of the institution's physical or academic architecture. Every major gift funds a Professorship, a Degree Program naming, or a Scholarship Fund that becomes a permanent line in the university's endowment.

The minimum founding gift that secures a named asset is $12,000 — a four-year scholarship for one student. The maximum is $25M — the naming of a Central Node in Venice, San Francisco, or Singapore.
