Executive Summary
The research portfolio, in one paragraph.

The University of Artemis conducts its research through nineteen Centers of Inquiry — distributed, mission-driven, and organized around the principal problems of the twenty-first century rather than around inherited academic disciplines. Each center operates across three nodes on three continents, is staffed by a core of tenured investigators and a larger cohort of fellows and postdocs, and is bound by the institution’s seven-year release policy: every center publishes its findings openly, on a fixed schedule, regardless of commercial potential.

This document is the complete catalog of the Artemis research portfolio. It describes the seven anchor centers that the founding campaign will fund directly — Synthetic Intelligence, Bio-Regenerative Arts, Cosmological Humanities, Urban Futures, Biotech & Life Sciences, Fintech/DeFi & Economics, and Health & Bioethics — with one-page entries detailing each center’s mission, node locations, investigator cohort, fellows cohort, and focus areas. It then catalogs the twelve additional centers that complete the nineteen-center portfolio, following the same structural template. It explains the seven-year release policy that governs all center output. It describes the translational programs — including the Forge incubator — that convert center research into deployed impact. And it describes the core-investigator model that governs how centers hire, promote, and retain the researchers who do the work.

The portfolio is deliberately planetary. Each center’s three nodes are chosen to span the geopolitical, economic, and ecological range of the problem it studies: a center on urban futures is distributed across Copenhagen, Medellín, and Singapore; a center on health is distributed across Boston, Cape Town, and Hyderabad. The distribution is not symbolic. It ensures that the research is informed by the lived conditions of the populations it most affects, and it ensures that no center becomes captive to the regulatory or political assumptions of a single jurisdiction. The nineteen-center portfolio, taken together, is the institutional commitment that Artemis does research for the planet, not for a single nation.

The seven-year release policy

Every Center of Inquiry is bound by the seven-year release policy: research output is published openly, on a fixed seven-year cycle, regardless of commercial potential. Centers may publish earlier — and most do — but no center may withhold a finding beyond the seven-year horizon. The policy is the institutional commitment that Artemis research exists for the public domain, not for the private balance sheet. It is also the structural feature that distinguishes Artemis from the venture-funded research models that have proliferated in the last decade, and from the patent-encumbered models of the previous century.

Chapter I
The seven-year release policy.

Every Center of Inquiry at Artemis operates under the seven-year release policy: research findings are published openly, on a fixed schedule, within seven years of the work being initiated. The policy applies to every center, every investigator, and every line of research funding the institution disperses. It is the structural commitment that Artemis research is conducted for the public domain.

The seven-year horizon was chosen as the longest defensible interval between the initiation of a research program and the publication of its findings. Shorter intervals are encouraged — the median time-to-publication across the founding centers is expected to be approximately three years — but no interval may exceed seven. The policy applies regardless of the commercial potential of the work: a center may not withhold a finding because a spin-out could be formed around it, may not withhold because a corporate partner has requested delay, and may not withhold because the institution itself wishes to monetize the result. The policy is binding, enforced by the Office of Research Integrity, and violation is grounds for the dissolution of the center.

The policy does not prohibit commercialization. Centers are free to file patents, form spin-outs, and engage in translational programs — and the institution operates the Forge incubator precisely to support such activity. But the patents are filed defensively, the spin-outs operate under open-licensing terms, and the translational programs are structured so that the research output reaches the public domain on the seven-year schedule regardless of the commercial trajectory. The institutional position is that commercialization and open release are compatible, and that the choice to commercialize does not confer the right to enclose.

A finding that cannot survive the daylight of public release is not a finding. It is a rumor held by a small group. Artemis does research, not rumors.
— From the Artemis Research Charter, Article 1

The first knowledge drop under the seven-year policy is scheduled for 2032 — seven years after the founding cohort of centers is activated in 2025. The drop will include the cumulative published output of the seven anchor centers, the institutional outcomes report on the founding research programs, and the external audit of compliance with the release policy. The drop will be open-access, indexed in the standard repositories, and accompanied by the underlying data and code wherever the consent of human subjects and the integrity of ongoing work permit. The institution has committed, in its charter, that no later knowledge drop will be delayed beyond the seven-year horizon, and that any delay beyond the median will be reported and explained in the institutional outcomes report.

The seven-year cycle
Initiate. Conduct. Publish. Repeat.
Each Center of Inquiry operates on a rolling seven-year cycle: research programs are initiated in year one, conducted through years two to five, published openly no later than year seven, and audited in the institutional outcomes report. Centers may publish earlier — the median time-to-publication across the founding centers is expected to be approximately three years — but no center may withhold a finding beyond the seven-year horizon. The cycle is the structural mechanism that ensures the institution’s research remains in the public domain.

Chapter II
The seven anchor centers.

The founding campaign funds seven anchor centers directly. Each is structured around a problem domain rather than an academic discipline, each operates across three nodes on three continents, and each is staffed by a core cohort of investigators and a larger cohort of fellows. The entries that follow document each center in the standard format.

The seven anchor centers were chosen to span the principal research frontiers of the century: machine intelligence, the built environment, the cosmos, the city, life itself, the economic substrate, and human health. The choice is not exhaustive — the twelve additional centers extend the portfolio into climate, energy, governance, narrative, and the foundations of mathematics — but the seven anchors are the institutional bet on where the most consequential work of the next quarter-century will be done. They are also the centers for which the founding campaign has secured the principal-investigator cohort, the node infrastructure, and the multi-year funding commitment.

Each Center of Inquiry operates across three nodes — one in each of three regions — ensuring the research is informed by the lived conditions of the populations it most affects.

The seven anchor centers were not chosen by committee. Each was proposed by a founding principal investigator who committed to lead the center for its first decade, who brought a research program of sufficient depth to justify the multi-year institutional commitment, and who could demonstrate that the program required the three-node, three-continent structure to be executed honestly. The institutional role was to vet the proposals — against the criterion of consequentiality, against the criterion of feasibility, and against the criterion of fit with the broader portfolio — and to provide the structural support. The centers are investigator-led institutions, not administrative units, and the investigators who proposed them are the directors who will lead them.

Chapter IV
The core-investigator model.

Every Center of Inquiry is staffed on the same model: a core cohort of tenured investigators, a larger cohort of fellows and postdocs, and a rotating population of visiting researchers drawn from partner institutions. The model is designed to give investigators the long horizon, the protected time, and the institutional stability required to do research that does not fit within a grant cycle.

The core-investigator cohort at each center is between 9 and 16 members at maturity, with the size calibrated to the center’s research program and the scale of its node infrastructure. Investigators are appointed to a center, not to a department, and their primary professional identity is the center rather than a disciplinary field. The appointment is structured as a long-term contract — ten years, renewable — with the institution committing to the funding of the investigator’s research program for the duration of the contract, regardless of the grant-funding environment. The structural commitment is that the investigator is freed from the grant cycle: the institution absorbs the funding risk, and the investigator is left to do the work.

The fellows cohort is larger — typically three to four times the investigator cohort — and is structured as a five-year postdoctoral appointment with explicit progression criteria. Fellows are recruited globally, are expected to spend time at each of the center’s three nodes during the appointment, and are supported toward independent investigator positions at Artemis or at partner institutions. The fellows cohort is the principal pipeline through which the center renews its investigator cadre, and the institution tracks the placement of every fellow as a measure of the center’s contribution to the broader research community.

The visiting-researcher program is the structural mechanism through which Artemis centers remain connected to the broader research community. Every center hosts a rotating population of visiting researchers — from partner institutions, from government, and from industry — on appointments of one semester to two years. The visiting program is funded by the institution, not by the visitor’s home organization, to ensure that the program is accessible to researchers from institutions without their own travel budgets. The program is also the principal channel through which the seven-year release policy is enforced: visiting researchers are bound by the same release terms as the core investigators, and the institution tracks the publication of work initiated during a visiting appointment.

What the core-investigator model rejects

The model rejects the grant-cycle research culture that has come to dominate the contemporary research university — the culture in which investigators spend a substantial fraction of their time pursuing short-term funding, in which research programs are calibrated to the grant cycle rather than to the question, and in which the institutional commitment to the investigator is contingent on the investigator’s continuing ability to attract external funding. The Artemis position is that this culture is incompatible with the long-horizon research the institution intends to conduct, and that the core-investigator model — with its ten-year contracts, its institutional funding commitment, and its release from the grant cycle — is the structural alternative.

The compensation model for investigators is published and transparent: a starting salary of $185,000 for a junior investigator, rising to $310,000 at the senior investigator level, with no negotiation on entry (the salary is fixed by rank) and a transparent sabbatical cycle of one year every seven years. The compensation is calibrated to be competitive with the leading research universities in the principal research economies, and the structural commitment is that the institution is competing for the same investigators as those universities, on a transparent and equitable basis. The compensation model is the institutional answer to the question of why a serious researcher would accept a long-term contract at a new institution: because the contract comes with the protected time, the long horizon, and the institutional stability that the grant-cycle culture cannot provide.

Chapter V
Center lifecycle & activation.

Every Center of Inquiry at Artemis follows the same lifecycle: proposal, vetting, activation, ramp-up, maturity, and external review. The lifecycle is the institutional mechanism that ensures each center earns its place in the portfolio, and that no center becomes a permanent entitlement regardless of its continuing contribution.

The proposal stage is open: any researcher affiliated with the institution may propose a new center, with a research program, a node plan, an investigator cohort, and a five-year budget. Proposals are vetted by the Research Portfolio Committee — a faculty body that includes the directors of the existing centers — against three criteria: consequentiality (does the proposal address a question of significance to the century?), feasibility (can the proposed team execute the program?), and fit (does the proposal complement or duplicate the existing portfolio?). Proposals that pass the vetting are forwarded to the board of trustees with a funding recommendation.

Activation begins with the recruitment of the founding investigator cohort and the establishment of the three nodes. The ramp-up phase — typically two to three years — covers the buildout of the node infrastructure, the recruitment of the fellows cohort, and the initiation of the first research programs. Maturity is reached when the center is operating at full headcount, with its research programs underway and its first publications approaching the seven-year release window. The external review — conducted every seven years by a panel of external researchers — evaluates whether the center has earned continuation, restructuring, or dissolution. The review is binding: a center that fails the review is dissolved, with its investigators offered transitions to other centers or to partner institutions.

The activation schedule

The seven anchor centers activate in 2025–2026, with the founding investigator cohort onboarded by Q2 2026 and the first research programs initiated by Q3 2026. The twelve additional centers activate on a rolling schedule between 2026 and 2030, with three to four centers activating per year. The full nineteen-center portfolio is operational by Q1 2030, in time for the institution’s first external review cycle to begin in 2032 — the same year as the first knowledge drop under the seven-year release policy.

Chapter VI
Translational programs & the Forge.

The Centers of Inquiry produce research; the translational programs convert that research into deployed impact. The principal translational vehicle is the Forge — the institutional incubator that supports the formation of spin-out ventures around center research, under open-licensing terms consistent with the seven-year release policy.

The Forge is structured as a non-equity incubator: it does not take ownership stakes in the ventures it supports, and the ventures are not a financial instrument of the institution. The Forge provides space, mentorship, regulatory navigation, and small amounts of seed funding — typically $50,000 to $250,000 per venture — in exchange for an open-licensing commitment on the underlying research and a royalty arrangement that returns a small percentage of venture revenue to the center that produced the research. The Forge is funded from the institutional surplus, not from external venture capital, and is structurally protected from the pressures that equity-based incubation would impose on the research agenda.

The Forge incubator supports spin-out ventures from across the nineteen centers, with a portfolio of approximately twenty ventures per year at maturity — each operating under open-licensing terms consistent with the seven-year release policy.

At maturity, the Forge will support a portfolio of approximately twenty ventures per year, drawn from across the nineteen centers. The portfolio is deliberately diversified across domains — biotech ventures, climate-tech ventures, AI ventures, fintech ventures — to ensure that the translational program does not become captive to the venture-funding environment of any single sector. The Forge publishes an annual outcomes report, audited externally, that tracks the survival, the revenue, the employment, and the open-licensing compliance of every venture in the portfolio. The report is the institutional commitment that the translational program is being measured by the same standards as the rest of the research enterprise.

Chapter VII
Governance, integrity, and the audit.

The Centers of Inquiry operate under the institutional research-integrity framework administered by the Office of Research Integrity. The framework governs the seven-year release policy, the conflict-of-interest regime, the open-data and open-code commitments, and the external audit of compliance.

The Office of Research Integrity is structurally independent of the centers it oversees. It reports to the board of trustees, not to the academic leadership, and its director is appointed for a single ten-year term to insulate the office from short-term pressures. The office conducts an annual audit of every center — reviewing the publication record, the conflict-of-interest disclosures, the open-data compliance, and the adherence to the seven-year release schedule — and publishes the audit findings in the institutional outcomes report. Centers that fail the audit are subject to corrective action, up to and including dissolution.

The conflict-of-interest regime is built on the principle of disclosure and structural separation. Investigators may hold equity in ventures spun out from their research, but the equity is held in a blind trust for the duration of the appointment and may not be traded on the basis of non-public information. Investigators may accept consulting fees from external organizations, but the consulting is capped at one day per week and the arrangements are disclosed in the public registry. The regime is not designed to prevent investigators from engaging with the world; it is designed to ensure that the engagement is visible and that the incentives do not corrupt the research.

The institutional outcomes report

Every five years, the institution publishes a research-outcomes report — audited externally, published openly — that catalogs the published output of every center, the compliance with the seven-year release policy, the disposition of every venture in the Forge portfolio, and the audit findings of the Office of Research Integrity. The report is the institutional commitment that the research enterprise is being measured by the same standards as the academic enterprise, and that the measurement is visible to the public that funds it.

The first research-outcomes report will be published in 2032, drawing on the cumulative output of the founding cohort of centers. Until then, the institution is judged, like its students and like its faculty, on the seriousness of the questions it has chosen to ask. The nineteen-center portfolio is the institutional answer: that the principal questions of the century are technical, ethical, and political at once; that they require a planetary distribution of expertise to address; and that they require a research enterprise bound by an open-release commitment to the public domain. The portfolio is the institutional commitment to that answer.
