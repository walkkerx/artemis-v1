Chapter 2
The Vision in Brief

A university without a physical place is a YouTube channel. A university with one place is a campus. A university with fifty places — wired into a single institution, governed as one, credentialed as one — is something the world has not yet built.

Artemis is designed to be that institution. It is one university, with one curriculum, one faculty, one degree, and one balance sheet — distributed across fifty colleges on six continents, anchored by three Central Nodes in Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore. A student who begins a degree in Kigali can complete a tutorial in Tokyo and defend a thesis in Venice, all within the same academic home. The campus is the planet.

The Artemis network at full build: 50 colleges on six continents, anchored by Central Nodes in Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore.

The conviction behind this design is straightforward. The great problems of the twenty-first century — climate, governance, intelligence, biosecurity, migration, the legitimacy of institutions — are planetary problems that happen to be administered nationally. A university built to address them cannot live in a single valley, however distinguished. It must be co-extensive with the territory of its questions. The university that outlives its founders also needs a corpus that outlives its donors; the financial model is engineered accordingly.

The Three Commitments

1. Access. $3,000/yr tuition — within reach of 90% of qualified students globally. 2. Unity. One curriculum, one faculty, one degree across all 50 colleges. 3. Permanence. Buildings owned, endowments endowed, self-sustaining within 12 months of launch.

The redesign rests on the Oxford-style tutorial method as the principal mode of instruction. Lectures are recorded and consumed asynchronously; the contact hour is the tutorial — one faculty member meeting two to four students weekly for sustained Socratic engagement. The four curriculum pillars (Epistemology, Computational Thinking, Global Systems, Creative Expression) are required of every graduate, regardless of discipline, in every college.

Chapter 4
The Three Central Nodes

Three cities anchor the network. Each hosts five thousand students. Each bears a founding donor's name in perpetuity. Together they form the governance, innovation, and convening triangle of the planetary university.

The Central Nodes are not administrative capitals in the conventional sense. They are the three places where the university convenes itself — where the founding document is archived, where convocation is held, where the Board of Visitors meets, where the president resides. Each Node hosts approximately five thousand students and is the largest single institution in its host city. Each is named by a founding donor at the $25M level.

Venice Central Node
The Founding Seat · ~5,000 students
The founding seat — governance, convocation, the founding document archive. Canals and Renaissance palazzi repurposed as the academic heart. Venice was chosen because it is the city where East meets West, where the Renaissance was financed, and where a planetary institution can convene without belonging to any single nation.
San Francisco Central Node
Technology & Innovation Seat · ~5,000 students
The technology and innovation seat — proximity to Silicon Valley, the Forge incubator, biotech and computational research. The San Francisco Node is where the institution's research portfolio meets the capital markets that can scale it, and where the 5% innovation equity carry that funds the operating budget is generated.
Singapore Central Node
Asia-Pacific Seat · ~5,000 students
The Asia-Pacific seat — a neutral convening point for the Eastern hemisphere, fintech and urban futures research. Singapore was chosen for its neutrality, its regulatory sophistication, and its position as the natural meeting point of South Asia, East Asia, and Oceania. A founding donor may name this Node for $25M.

Beyond the three Central Nodes, the network comprises eight Tier A colleges (~2,500 students each, in London, Tokyo, Berlin, Paris, Toronto, Sydney, Seoul, New York), sixteen Tier B colleges (~2,000 students each, in Nairobi, Mumbai, São Paulo, Cairo, Istanbul, Buenos Aires, Jakarta, Mexico City, and others), and twenty-three Tier C colleges (~1,100 students each, in Kigali, Dhaka, Kampala, Karachi, Tashkent, Quito, and emerging knowledge cities). Fifty colleges. Sixty thousand students. One institution.

Chapter 5
Naming Opportunities

Every gift at every level carries a name. From a $12K student scholarship to a $25M Central Node, the naming architecture is designed so that no founding contribution is invisible — and so that every named asset is permanent.

Artemis's naming philosophy is simple: a named gift is not a plaque, it is an endowment. When you name a College, the College owns the building; your name is attached to that building for as long as the institution exists. When you name a Professorship, your gift is invested at 4.5% yield, and the income funds one faculty position in perpetuity. There is no temporary naming, no five-year term, no expiry.

Chapter 6

Six circles, structured by gift size, each carrying a defined set of permanent recognitions. The circles exist to honour contribution at every scale — from $99 to $25M+ — and to ensure that every founding donor is part of the founding community.

Membership in a circle is determined by cumulative lifetime giving during the founding campaign, and membership is permanent — recorded in the founding charter, on the founding Donor Roll, and, at the higher levels, in physical engravings at the Central Nodes. The circles are not a loyalty program; they are a permanent record of who built the institution, and at what level of commitment.

Chapter 7
Donor Segments — How Campaigns Work

Founding campaigns do not raise money from crowds. They raise money from a small number of people who understand that institutions are built before they are announced. The Artemis campaign follows the standard distribution: 70% from 10 donors, 20% from 20 donors, 10% from hundreds.

This distribution is not a preference — it is a structural fact about how institutions are founded. Yale's "For Humanity" campaign secured $3.5 billion in its quiet phase, before any public announcement, from fewer than fifty donors. Stanford's campaign raised more than 70% of its goal from lead donors before the public launch. The pattern is consistent because the logic is consistent: lead donors commit privately because their commitment is what makes the public campaign credible.

The arithmetic is straightforward. Ten lead donors at an average of $7M each secure seventy percent of the campaign. Twenty major donors at an average of $1M each secure another twenty percent. This last tranche is small in dollars but large in signal: a broad founding community is itself a form of credibility.

Chapter 11
Tax Deductibility

Artemis is structured to provide tax-efficient giving in twelve major donor jurisdictions, covering approximately 85% of global philanthropic capacity. The table below summarises the vehicle, the deductibility, and the key notes for each jurisdiction.

Artemis is incorporated as three parallel legal entities: a Delaware non-stock 501(c)(3) public charity (primary), an England & Wales Charitable Incorporated Organisation (secondary), and a Swiss Fondation (tertiary). Until the 501(c)(3) determination letter is issued, Artemis operates under fiscal sponsorship through Rockefeller Philanthropy Advisors, NGOsource, and Global Impact, providing immediate US tax deductibility. Detailed tax guidance — including for appreciated assets, securities, and cryptocurrency — is available in the separate Artemis Tax Guide.

Chapter 13
The Ask

We are asking for one hundred million dollars from approximately thirty donors to build a university that will, within a decade, generate a five-hundred-and-forty-three-million-dollar endowment and educate sixty thousand students per year. We are asking you to be one of those thirty.

The ask is precise. The campaign requires ten lead gifts at an average of $7M each, and twenty major gifts at an average of $1M each. Every lead gift is structured around a named asset — a Central Node, a College, a Center of Inquiry, or a Distinguished Professorship — that becomes a permanent part of the institution's physical or academic architecture. Every major gift funds a Professorship, a Degree Program naming, or a Scholarship Fund that becomes a permanent line in the university's endowment.

The minimum founding gift that secures a named asset is $12,000 — a four-year scholarship for one student. The maximum is $25M — the naming of a Central Node in Venice, San Francisco, or Singapore. Between these two figures there are seven distinct naming opportunities, each with its own financial structure, its own recognition, and its own place in the founding architecture. There is a path for every serious founding donor.

The institution you seed in 2025 will, by 2035, be one of the better-endowed universities in the world. The students it educates will, by 2055, be the leaders of the institutions that succeed the ones we know today. The only thing required is that the first ten donors say yes.
— The Founding Cabinet
