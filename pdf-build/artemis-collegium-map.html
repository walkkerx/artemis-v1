At a Glance
A planetary footprint, day one.

The University of Artemis opens its doors with fifty Colleges across thirty-five countries and six continents, anchored by three Central Nodes in Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore. No university in history has opened with a planetary footprint of this scale; the founding configuration is itself the institutional proposition. This document is the geographic guide to that footprint — the atlas of the planetary university, organized continent by continent, city by city.

The Collegium Alliance is the formal name of the planetary network. Each College is a physical campus, owned in fee simple by the institution, hosting between 1,100 and 2,500 students in the standard Artemis curriculum. The three Central Nodes are the apex — each hosting approximately 5,000 students and serving as the regional seat of governance, convocation, and the founding document archive. Together, the fifty Colleges and three Central Nodes form a single institution operating across fifty-three physical locations, with a single curriculum, a single faculty roster, and a single treasury.

The geographic distribution of the Alliance is the result of three principles: continental balance (every inhabited continent hosts at least one College, and no continent hosts more than a third of the total); urban concentration (every College is in a city, not a greenfield campus, because the institution is urbanist by design); and access calibration (the tier framework ensures that the cities the existing university system has underserved are represented alongside the cities it has historically served). The map is therefore neither a list of the world's most prestigious academic cities nor a list of the world's most underserved; it is a calibrated selection that makes the planetary university planetary.

Chapter I
Why a planetary map — the geographic logic.

A university that calls itself planetary must be planetary in its geography, not merely in its aspirations. The map of the Collegium Alliance is the proof that the institution's claim to be planetary is not metaphorical. Fifty Colleges in thirty-five countries, across six continents, anchored by three Central Nodes — the geographic distribution is the institutional proposition made visible.

The planetary map exists for three reasons. The first is structural: a university that operates in a single country is, regardless of its curriculum, a national institution — subject to that country's regulatory regime, political climate, and diplomatic posture. A university that operates across thirty-five countries is structurally protected against the capture that any single country could attempt. The planetary map is therefore the institutional defense against political risk: distributed geography is the structural condition for institutional independence.

The second reason is pedagogical. A planetary university must produce graduates who have lived and studied in multiple cultural contexts, who understand the planetary scale of the problems they will be asked to solve, and who carry the network's perspective into their careers. A single-campus university, however distinguished, cannot produce this experience; the planetary map is the pedagogical instrument by which the institution produces planetary graduates. The student who studies in Kampala, spends a term in Venice, and graduates from a tutorial in Singapore has been formed by a curriculum that no single-campus institution can deliver.

The third reason is access. The existing global university system, despite its many achievements, serves approximately 5% of the world's college-age population at the quality tier that the leading research universities represent. The remaining 95% are served by national systems of variable quality, or not served at all. A planetary university that places Colleges in Kigali, Dhaka, and Kampala is making a structural commitment to access that a university concentrated in the global North cannot make. The map is therefore the access mission made geographic — the cities the existing system has failed are not an afterthought but a founding priority.

The planetary network at night — the lights of the cities the Collegium Alliance is embedded in. The map is not a list of the world's most prestigious academic cities; it is a calibrated selection that makes the planetary university planetary.

The planetary map is also the institutional answer to the question of scale. The traditional research university scales by adding students to a single campus until the campus is full, then stops scaling. The Artemis network scales by adding Colleges to the network, each one a campus of 1,100 to 2,500 students, with the marginal cost of each new College falling as the shared infrastructure (curriculum, faculty, digital platform) is already in place. The map is therefore the scaling architecture of the institution — the geographic form of the network's compounding advantage.

The three reasons the map matters

Structural: distributed geography is the institutional defense against political capture. Pedagogical: the planetary experience is the curriculum that no single-campus institution can deliver. Access: the cities the existing system has failed are a founding priority, not an afterthought. The map is the proof that the planetary university is planetary.

Chapter II
The three Central Nodes — the apex cities.

The three Central Nodes — Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore — are the apex of the planetary network. Each Node serves as the regional seat of governance, convocation, and the founding document archive for one third of the planetary university. The Nodes are the structural mechanism by which the institution's governance is distributed across three legally and culturally distinct jurisdictions, preventing capture by any single national government.

Venice anchors the European and Mediterranean basin. The founding seat of the institution, Venice houses the founding document archive, the convocation hall where the first graduating class is sworn in, and the historical identity of the institution. The Venice Node occupies repurposed Renaissance palazzi along the Grand Canal — acquired, restored, and operated as the academic heart of the European basin. The city's geographic isolation from any single national capital, its historical role as the meeting point between East and West, and its architectural inheritance make it the natural seat for an institution whose identity is planetary rather than national.

San Francisco anchors the Americas and the Pacific technology arc. The innovation seat of the institution, San Francisco houses the Forge incubator, the computational and biotech research programs, and the technology charter. The San Francisco Node occupies a repurposed industrial complex in the Mission Bay innovation district, with proximity to Silicon Valley, the biotech corridor, and the venture capital community. The Bay Area's existing research universities (Stanford, Berkeley, UCSF) provide a collegial ecosystem of complementary research capacity, with collaboration pathways in biotech, computational science, and adjacent fields.

Chapter III
Europe — the founding basin.

Europe is the founding basin of the planetary university. The Venice Central Node anchors the European presence; the Tier A Colleges in London, Berlin, and Paris extend the network into the continent's three knowledge capitals; and Tier B Colleges in Istanbul and Casablanca carry the network into the European-Mediterranean frontier. The European footprint is both the historical foundation of the institution and the gateway to the Mediterranean, North Africa, and the Middle East.

Europe's eight Colleges serve approximately 17,000 students across five countries. The Venice Central Node hosts 5,000; the three Tier A Colleges (London, Berlin, Paris) host 7,500 combined; the Tier B Colleges in Istanbul and Casablanca host 4,000 combined; and the Tier C Colleges in Tashkent and Yerevan host approximately 2,200 combined. The European footprint is calibrated to the continent's existing research capacity — the planet's most concentrated university ecosystem — while extending the network into the cities where the existing system is weakest.

Venice is the founding seat because Venice was, for five centuries, the original planetary city — the meeting point between the Latin West, the Byzantine East, and the Islamic South, whose identity was defined by its capacity to convene across cultural and religious boundaries. The planetary university inherits that vocation. London, Berlin, and Paris are the three European cities whose research universities have most defined the modern academy; the planetary network cannot be planetary without a permanent, owned, named presence in each of them. Istanbul is the bridge between Europe and Asia, the historical capital of two empires, and the city whose contemporary identity most embodies the planetary university's claim to convene across civilizational boundaries.

Casablanca carries the network into the Maghreb, with a Tier B College serving as the institution's gateway to North Africa and the francophone Mediterranean. The Casablanca placement is calibrated to the city's status as the economic capital of Morocco, the largest French-speaking city in the Maghreb, and the natural hub for the institution's outreach to francophone West Africa. Tashkent and Yerevan complete the eastern European reach — cities whose existing university systems are recovering from the post-Soviet transition, and where the planetary university's $3,000 tuition and need-blind admission will have the largest marginal impact on the access pipeline.

The European strategic significance

Europe is the founding basin not because Europe is the center of the planetary university — the planetary university has no center — but because the European-Mediterranean basin is the historical origin of the planetary idea. The Venice Central Node anchors the institution in that history; the London, Berlin, and Paris Tier A Colleges extend it into the present; and the Istanbul, Casablanca, Tashkent, and Yerevan Colleges carry it into the future.

Chapter IV
Asia-Pacific — the demographic center of gravity.

Asia-Pacific is the demographic center of gravity of the planetary university. The Singapore Central Node anchors the Asia-Pacific presence; the Tier A Colleges in Tokyo and Seoul extend the network into the continent's two leading knowledge economies; and Tier B Colleges in Mumbai, Jakarta, Bangkok, Hanoi, and Manila carry the network into the cities whose economic gravity shapes the sub-continent. The Asia-Pacific footprint is the network's largest and most demographically consequential.

Asia-Pacific's eleven Colleges serve approximately 22,600 students across nine countries. The Singapore Central Node hosts 5,000; the two Tier A Colleges (Tokyo, Seoul) host 5,000 combined; the five Tier B Colleges (Mumbai, Jakarta, Bangkok, Hanoi, Manila) host 10,000 combined; and the four Tier C Colleges (Dhaka, Karachi, Colombo, Kathmandu) host approximately 4,400 combined. The Asia-Pacific footprint is calibrated to the region's demographic weight — half of the world's college-age population lives in Asia — and to the region's strategic centrality to the twenty-first century.

The Cairo and Lagos Tier B Colleges host 4,000 combined; the Nairobi and Accra Tier B Colleges host 4,000 combined; and the Kigali, Kampala, and Addis Ababa Tier C Colleges host approximately 3,300 combined. The African footprint is calibrated to the continent's demographic trajectory — Africa will host 32% of the world's college-age population by 2050 — and to the access mission that is the institution's founding commitment.

The access frontier

Africa will host 32% of the world's college-age population by 2050. The seven Colleges of the African footprint are calibrated to that demographic trajectory — with the Tier B commercial capitals (Cairo, Lagos, Nairobi, Accra) anchoring the regional presence and the Tier C access cities (Kigali, Kampala, Addis Ababa) extending the network into the cities the existing system has most underserved.

The African footprint is also the institutional commitment to the access mission that the founding document articulates. The planetary university's $3,000 tuition is calibrated to be accessible to 90% of qualified students worldwide; the remaining 10% require scholarship assistance. In Africa, the percentage requiring scholarship assistance is higher — closer to 30% — because the continent's per-capita income, while rising rapidly, remains below the global median. The African Colleges are therefore the institutional commitment that the access mission is not a marketing claim but a structural feature of the network's geographic distribution.

A planetary university that has no presence in Australia is, by definition, not planetary; the Sydney Tier A College is the institutional answer to that requirement.

Chapter X
Northern and Southern hemispheres — the planetary balance.

The planetary university is calibrated to balance the Northern and Southern hemispheres — the demographic weight of the global North with the demographic trajectory of the global South. The fifty-three campuses of the founding configuration are distributed across both hemispheres, with the Northern hemisphere hosting the majority (reflecting the planet's population distribution) and the Southern hemisphere hosting a calibrated minority (reflecting the access mission's commitment to the global South).

The Northern hemisphere hosts 41 of the 53 campuses — approximately 77% of the planetary footprint. This distribution reflects the planet's population: roughly 88% of the world's population lives in the Northern hemisphere. The Northern footprint is concentrated in Europe (8 Colleges), Asia-Pacific (11 Colleges), North America (5 Colleges), and the northern portions of Africa and the Middle East. The Northern hemisphere's research capacity — the planet's most capitalized university ecosystem — is also the institutional context in which the founding configuration operates.

The Southern hemisphere hosts 12 of the 53 campuses — approximately 23% of the planetary footprint. This distribution is calibrated to the access mission: the Southern hemisphere hosts a disproportionate share of the planetary university's Tier C Colleges, reflecting the institution's commitment to the cities the existing university system has most underserved. The Southern footprint is concentrated in Latin America (8 Colleges), Oceania (2 Colleges), and the southern portions of Africa (2 Colleges). The Sydney Tier A College serves as the Southern hemisphere's flagship presence — the institutional commitment that the Southern hemisphere is represented in the network's apex tier.

The Northern-Southern balance is also a temporal balance. The Northern hemisphere's existing university systems are mature — the planet's most capitalized research infrastructure, the most distinguished research traditions, and the most established academic cultures. The Southern hemisphere's university systems are emerging — with the demographic trajectory, the economic growth, and the intellectual ambition that will define the twenty-first century. The planetary university's calibration — 77% Northern, 23% Southern — is the institutional commitment to be present in both the mature and the emerging research ecosystems, and to bridge them through the network's shared curriculum and faculty rotation.

The calibration is also reflected in the convocation calendar. The three Central Nodes — Venice (Northern), San Francisco (Northern), and Singapore (Northern) — are all in the Northern hemisphere, reflecting the planet's demographic distribution. The annual convocation calendar — Venice in October, San Francisco in February, Singapore in June — is calibrated to the Northern hemisphere's academic year, with the founding cohort admitted in the Northern autumn. The Southern hemisphere's academic year (which begins in the Southern autumn) is integrated into the network's calendar through the flexibility of the tutorial system and the asynchronous video library that permits every lecture to be reviewed by every student across the network.

Chapter XI
How students move between continents.

The planetary map is not a static catalog of campuses but a living network through which students, faculty, and ideas move continuously. Every Artemis student is entitled to spend at least one academic term at a node other than their home College, with tuition, credit, and financial aid flowing seamlessly across the network. The mobility program is the pedagogical instrument by which the planetary map becomes the planetary experience.

The mobility program is structured around three tiers of movement. The first tier is the term-abroad program: every Artemis student is entitled to spend one term at a node other than their home College, with the cost of travel, housing, and tuition covered by the operating P&L (not by an additional fee). The term-abroad program is the institutional commitment that the planetary experience is available to every student, regardless of financial circumstance. A student who enters at the Kampala College may spend a term at the Venice Central Node; a student who enters at the Singapore Central Node may spend a term at the São Paulo College.

The second tier is the multi-node pathway: students who demonstrate the academic preparation and the personal maturity may construct a multi-node pathway that takes them through three or more Colleges across their degree. The multi-node pathway is the institutional mechanism for producing the planetary graduate — the student who has been formed by the curriculum of multiple continents, who carries the network's perspective into their career, and who serves as the institutional ambassador to the next generation of applicants. The multi-node pathway is competitive, with approximately 20% of each cohort participating, and is the institutional commitment to producing graduates whose formation is genuinely planetary.

The mobility program in practice — students from the Kampala College spend a term at the Venice Central Node, students from the Singapore Node spend a term at the São Paulo College, and the network's shared curriculum ensures that credit, tuition, and financial aid flow seamlessly across the planetary footprint.

The third tier is the research rotation: graduate students and senior undergraduates are entitled to spend research terms at any of the institution's nineteen Centers of Inquiry, which are distributed across multiple nodes. A graduate student working on the Synthetic Intelligence Center may spend a term at San Francisco (the lead node), a term at Toronto (the second node), and a term at Edinburgh (the third node) — accumulating the research experience that is the credential's distinctive feature at the graduate level. The research rotation is funded by the Center's endowment, not by the student's home College, ensuring that the planetary research experience is available to every graduate student regardless of their home institution.

The mobility program is the pedagogical answer to the question that the planetary map poses. A university that operates fifty-three campuses across thirty-five countries could, in principle, be a federation of fifty-three independent institutions — each campus a separate university, sharing only a name. The mobility program is the institutional commitment that the Collegium Alliance is not a federation but a single institution: a student who enters at any College carries the institution's degree, the institution's curriculum, and the institution's network into their career. The map is therefore not only geographic but pedagogical — the architecture by which the planetary university produces planetary graduates.

The three tiers of movement

Term-abroad: one term at a non-home node, funded from the operating P&L. Multi-node pathway: three or more Colleges across the degree, competitive admission, approximately 20% participation. Research rotation: terms at any of the 19 Centers of Inquiry, funded by the Center's endowment. Together, these three tiers convert fifty-three campuses into a single pedagogical institution.

Chapter XII
The second wave — decade-two expansion cities.

The fifty-three campuses of Year 1 are the founding configuration, not the final configuration. The institution's design horizon extends to a century and beyond, with the network architecture permitting expansion at marginal cost as new cities are added. This chapter previews the second-wave cities that will join the Collegium Alliance in the decade following the founding — the next tier of placements that will extend the planetary footprint into the regions the founding fifty does not reach.

The second-wave priorities are organized by the same three principles that guide the founding footprint: continental balance, urban concentration, and access calibration. In sub-Saharan Africa, the second wave targets Accra (already in the founding footprint as a Tier B), Addis Ababa (already in the founding footprint as a Tier C), Dakar, Dar es Salaam, Lusaka, and Maputo — cities whose demographic trajectory and existing research ecosystems make them the natural second-decade placements. In South Asia, the second wave targets Colombo and Kathmandu (already in the founding footprint as Tier C), plus Chandigarh, Pune, and Chittagong — cities whose population and economic gravity make them the natural Indian-subcontinent second wave.

In Latin America, the second wave targets Medellín (already in the founding footprint as a Tier C), Curitiba, Monterrey, Valparaíso, and La Paz — cities whose existing research traditions and demographic weight make them the natural Latin American second wave. In Southeast Asia, the second wave targets Ho Chi Minh City, Surabaya, and Yangon — cities whose demographic trajectory and economic integration make them the natural Southeast Asian second wave. In Europe, the second wave targets Edinburgh (already a node of the Synthetic Intelligence Center), Copenhagen (already a node of the Urban Futures Center), Madrid, Vienna, and Dublin — cities whose existing research ecosystems make them the natural European second wave.

The marginal cost of expansion

The marginal cost of adding the fifty-fourth College is on the order of $5M — acquisition, renovation, and integration into the existing network. The shared curriculum, faculty, and digital infrastructure are already in place. The second wave is therefore not a fundraising challenge but an operational one: the institution adds Colleges as the founding configuration stabilizes, the endowment grows, and the access mission's next tier of cities is identified.

The second wave is sequenced to maintain the founding commitment: governance first, then flagship presence, then regional capital, then access. The second decade is weighted toward access — the cities the founding fifty does not reach, with the planetary university's $3,000 tuition and need-blind admission continuing to expand the access pipeline. The second wave is also weighted toward the Southern hemisphere, with five of the twelve priority second-decade cities in the Southern hemisphere — extending the planetary balance toward the demographic trajectory of the global South.

The century-scale design horizon is the institutional commitment that motivates the second wave. The fifty-three campuses of Year 1 are the seed; the second wave is the institution's first decade of growth; the century ahead is the institution's actual horizon. The Collegium Alliance Map is therefore not a fixed catalog but a living document — the atlas of an institution that intends to grow into the century it enters, and that calibrates its geographic footprint to the demographic, scientific, and access priorities of each decade it lives through.
