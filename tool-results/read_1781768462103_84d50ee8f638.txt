   157→  <div class="chapter-header">
   158→    <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter One · Foundations</div>
   159→    <h2 class="chapter-title">Why Giving Circles</h2>
   160→    <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   161→  </div>
   162→
   163→  <p class="lead">A giving circle is not a pricing tier. It is a community of recognition — a way for a university to say, with permanence and precision, that this person, at this level of commitment, helped to build what now stands.</p>
   164→
   165→  <p class="body-text">The University of Artemis has structured its founding campaign around six giving circles, each defined by a commitment threshold and each carrying a specific set of recognitions, privileges, and community memberships. The circles are not transactional: a donor does not purchase a benefit by giving a particular amount. Rather, the circles are the institutional vocabulary by which the university acknowledges that different scales of commitment make different scales of impact possible, and that the appropriate recognition for a $50,000 commitment is not the same as the appropriate recognition for a $10 million commitment. To treat them as identical would be to dishonor both.</p>
   166→
   167→  <p class="body-text">The six circles range from the Founders' Circle at $10 million and above, through the Guardians' Circle ($5 million to $9.9 million), the Builders' Circle ($1 million to $4.9 million), the Fellows' Circle ($100,000 to $999,000), and Friends of Artemis ($10,000 to $99,000), down to The 99 at $99 — the entry-level commitment that establishes a place on the waitlist for enrollment and a permanent mark on the founding community wall. Each circle has its own accent color, its own recognition package, and its own annual rhythm of events and communications. Together, the six circles compose the full founding community: 360 individuals and institutions whose commitments, large and small, have built the university from its first charter to its first academic year.</p>
   168→
   169→  <div class="pullquote">
   170→    "The size of a gift is not a measure of the donor's worth. It is a measure of the responsibility the university accepts in their name."
   171→    <div class="attribution">— Charter of the Founding Campaign, University of Artemis, 2025</div>
   172→  </div>
   173→
   174→  <p class="body-text">Three principles govern the design of the circles. The first is <strong>permanence</strong>: every recognition, from the engraved name on the founding document to the digital mark on the community wall, is intended to outlast the donor's lifetime and the university's first century. The founding community is being recorded as a permanent historical fact, not as a fundraising tactic. The second is <strong>community</strong>: each circle is not merely a list of names but a network of donors who share a common commitment to the university's mission and who meet, correspond, and collaborate through the circle's annual events. The third is <strong>proportion</strong>: the recognitions, privileges, and access associated with each circle are calibrated to the scale of the commitment, ensuring that no donor receives either too little or too much acknowledgment for what they have given.</p>
   175→
   176→  <p class="body-text">The chapters that follow describe each circle in detail. For each, you will find the commitment range, the recognition package, the annual events and communications, the community composition, and the strategic role that the circle plays within the broader founding campaign. Donors considering a commitment at any level should read the chapter for the circle they expect to enter and the chapter for the circle above it, since the recognition packages are designed to be aspirational rather than transactional — a donor considering a $1 million commitment should understand both what Builders' Circle recognition includes and what Guardians' Circle recognition would add, in case the donor's circumstances permit a larger commitment.</p>

   227→  <p class="body-text">Three observations frame the structure of the six circles. First, the threshold amounts are calibrated to the recognitions offered: the Founders' Circle, which confers a permanent seat on the Board of Visitors and naming of an endowment fund, requires a commitment sufficient to endow a substantial programme in perpetuity — $10 million produces approximately $400,000 in annual spendable income at a 4% spending policy, enough to fund a named professorship, a scholarship fund, or a small center of inquiry. Second, the circles are designed to be inclusive at the lower end and exclusive at the upper end: The 99 admits anyone willing to make a $99 commitment, while the Founders' Circle will likely include fewer than twenty-five individuals worldwide at the close of the founding campaign. Third, the recognitions compound across circles — a Founders' Circle donor receives all of the recognitions offered to the lower circles, plus the Founders' Circle-specific privileges.</p>

   234→  <p class="body-text">The founding community — the donors across all six circles whose commitments are recorded during the founding campaign — will be permanently recorded in the university's founding documents, in the inscription at the Venice Central Node, and in the digital founding records maintained by the Office of Gift Planning. Donors who give after the close of the founding campaign will be recognised as annual donors rather than founding donors, regardless of the size of their commitment. The founding window is the period between the university's initial capitalisation in 2025 and the conclusion of the founding campaign, anticipated in 2028; donors uncertain whether their commitment will qualify for founding recognition should confirm with the Office of Gift Planning before making their gift.</p>

   236→  <!-- CHAPTER 3: FOUNDERS' CIRCLE -->
   237→  <div class="circle-header" style="border-left-color:#8A0000;">
   238→    <div class="ch-tier">Circle 01 · Highest Tier</div>
   239→    <div class="ch-name">The Founders' Circle</div>

   242→
   243→  <p class="body-text">The Founders' Circle is the highest tier of recognition in the University of Artemis founding campaign. It is reserved for donors whose commitments reach or exceed $10 million — a threshold calibrated to the cost of endowing a substantial academic programme in perpetuity. A $10 million endowment at a 4% spending policy produces approximately $400,000 in annual spendable income, sufficient to fund a named professorship, a scholarship programme supporting 10 to 20 students annually, or the operating costs of a small center of inquiry. The Founders' Circle will likely include fewer than twenty-five individuals, families, and foundations at the close of the founding campaign, making it the most exclusive community within the university's founding record.</p>
   244→
   245→  <p class="body-text">To enter the Founders' Circle is to become a permanent fixture of the university's institutional memory. Founders' Circle donors have their names engraved on the university's founding document at the Venice Central Node — a physical inscription intended to endure for the lifetime of the institution and visible to every student, faculty member, and visitor who passes through the central node. They receive a permanent seat on the Board of Visitors, the advisory body that provides strategic counsel to the President and the Board of Directors. They are invited to a private annual dinner with the President, hosted at a rotating Central Node (Venice, London, or the third Central Node), where the year's strategic developments are presented and discussed. They may name an endowment fund or an equivalent naming opportunity within the university, choosing from a portfolio of available professorships, scholarships, programs, and capital projects.</p>
   246→
   247→  <p class="body-text">Beyond the formal recognitions, the Founders' Circle is a community of peers. The donors in this circle share a level of commitment to the university's mission that few others in the world will match; their annual dinners become forums not only for institutional updates but for substantive conversation about the future of higher education, the challenges facing the next generation of students, and the strategic priorities that the university should pursue in the decade ahead. Founders' Circle donors frequently become the university's most trusted advisors on questions of strategy, governance, and external relations — roles that the President and Board of Directors cultivate deliberately through individual briefings, private consultations, and inclusion in the university's most consequential decisions.</p>

   435→  <div class="chapter-header">
   436→    <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter Nine · The Architecture of Recognition</div>
   437→    <h2 class="chapter-title">Recognition, Community, Legacy</h2>
   438→    <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   439→  </div>
   440→
   441→  <p class="lead">The six giving circles are the architecture through which the University of Artemis constructs three things at once: the recognition of individual donors, the formation of a community among them, and the legacy they collectively leave to the institution they have built.</p>
   442→
   443→  <p class="body-text">Recognition is the most visible of the three. Each circle carries a defined package of recognitions — engraved names, named entities, board seats, lectures, certificates, plaques, and entries on the Donor Roll — that publicly records the donor's commitment and its institutional significance. The recognitions are designed to be permanent: the engraved name at the Venice Central Node is intended to endure for the lifetime of the institution; the named College or professorship operates in perpetuity under the donor's name; the Donor Roll is maintained as a permanent historical record. Recognition at the University of Artemis is not a fundraising tactic but a historical inscription — the university's formal acknowledgment that this person, at this level, helped to build what now stands.</p>
   444→
   445→  <p class="body-text">Community is the second function of the circles. Each circle is a network of donors who share a common level of commitment to the university's mission and who meet, correspond, and collaborate through the circle's annual events, briefings, and communications. The Founders' Circle meets annually at a rotating Central Node for a private dinner with the President. The Guardians' Circle meets annually in conjunction with the President's briefing. The Builders' Circle gathers annually at one of the Central Nodes. The Fellows' Circle meets at regional events in major cities. Friends of Artemis and The 99 convene through online events and monthly or quarterly communications. The community function is what transforms a list of donors into a network of peers — people who know one another, who share substantive interests, and who together constitute the founding community of the university.</p>
   446→
   447→  <div class="figure">
   448→    <img src="../public/resources/img/graduation.png" alt="Graduation ceremony">
   449→    <div class="caption">Every giving circle exists for this moment — the day a student crosses the stage and becomes an alumnus of the institution the donors built. Recognition records the donor; community sustains them; legacy outlives them.</div>
   450→  </div>
   451→
   452→  <p class="body-text">Legacy is the third and most consequential function. A donor's commitment to the University of Artemis is recorded not only in the recognitions and the community of their own circle but in the permanent architecture of the institution they have helped to build. The Founders' Circle donor whose name is engraved on the founding document is remembered as long as the university endures. The Guardians' Circle donor whose name is attached to a College is remembered by every student who passes through that College. The Builders' Circle donor whose name is attached to a scholarship is remembered by every student who receives it. The Friends of Artemis donor whose name is on the Donor Roll is remembered as part of the founding community. The donor in The 99 is remembered as one of the first to declare their support. Legacy is what remains when the donor is gone; the circles are the architecture by which the university ensures that something remains.</p>
   453→
   454→  <p class="body-text">The three functions — recognition, community, legacy — operate at every tier of the giving circles, but they operate differently at each tier. At the Founders' Circle, recognition is institutional (a Board seat, an engraved name), community is intimate (a small group of peers meeting annually with the President), and legacy is architectural (the foundation on which the university itself rests). At The 99, recognition is collective (a place on the community wall), community is broad (a global network of early supporters), and legacy is declarative (the assertion that this donor was first in line). The difference is one of scale, not of kind: every donor, at every tier, is recognised, is part of a community, and leaves a legacy. The circles are the structure through which the university ensures that all three functions are discharged with appropriate proportion to the commitment that activated them.</p>

   459→  </div>
   460→
   461→  <p class="body-text">Donors considering a commitment at any tier of the giving circles are encouraged to think beyond the recognition package to the community and legacy functions that the commitment will activate. The recognition package is what the donor receives in the year of the gift; the community is what the donor becomes part of for the years that follow; the legacy is what the donor leaves to the institution and to the students who will inhabit it for generations. The recognition package is documented in the chapters above; the community is experienced through the events, briefings, and communications described therein; the legacy is the permanent architectural fact of the donor's name on the founding record of a planetary university. The Office of Gift Planning is available to discuss any of these dimensions with any donor at any time, and to facilitate the donor's engagement with the community and the legacy appropriate to their commitment.</p>

   465→    <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter Ten · Practical Steps</div>
   466→    <h2 class="chapter-title">How to Enter a Circle</h2>
   467→    <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   468→  </div>
   469→
   470→  <p class="lead">Entering a giving circle is a formal act: a documented commitment, a recognition agreement, and an entry in the university's founding records. The process is designed to be substantive rather than transactional.</p>
   471→
   472→  <p class="body-text">The process of entering a giving circle begins with a conversation. Donors considering a commitment at any tier are encouraged to contact the Office of Gift Planning to discuss their interests, the recognition package appropriate to their commitment level, and the timing of the gift relative to the founding campaign's closing window. For commitments at the Founders' Circle and Guardians' Circle levels, this conversation typically involves the President or a senior gift officer and may extend over several meetings as the donor's strategic interests, recognition preferences, and structuring options are explored. For commitments at the Builders' Circle and Fellows' Circle levels, the conversation is typically conducted by a senior gift officer and may be completed in a single meeting. For commitments at Friends of Artemis and The 99, the conversation is conducted through the university's online giving portal and may be completed in a single session.</p>
   473→
   474→  <p class="body-text">Following the initial conversation, the donor and the university execute a written gift agreement that documents the commitment amount, the payment schedule (immediate, multi-year pledge, or deferred), the recognition package, and any restrictions on the use of the gift. The gift agreement is the formal instrument by which the donor's commitment is recorded and the university's recognition obligations are established. For commitments at the Founders' Circle and Guardians' Circle levels, the gift agreement is typically a bespoke document drafted by counsel for the donor and reviewed by the university's Office of the General Counsel. For commitments at lower tiers, the gift agreement is the university's standard form, available through the Office of Gift Planning. Donors are encouraged to review the gift agreement with their own legal and tax advisors before signing.</p>
   475→
   476→  <p class="body-text">Upon execution of the gift agreement and receipt of the initial payment, the donor is formally entered into the appropriate giving circle and the recognition package is activated. The donor's name is added to the founding Donor Roll, the appropriate naming opportunity (if any) is reserved, the engraved recognition (if any) is scheduled for installation, and the donor's entry into the founding community is recorded in the university's permanent records. The Office of Gift Planning provides the donor with written confirmation of the entry into the circle and a schedule of the recognition activations, including the dates by which each recognition element will be delivered. Donors may request updates on the status of their recognition activations at any time.</p>

   492→  <p class="body-text">A note on multi-year pledges. Donors may structure their commitment as a multi-year pledge — typically over three to five years, though longer periods are negotiable for Founders' Circle and Guardians' Circle commitments — with the circle entry activated upon execution of the gift agreement and the initial payment. The recognitions are activated when the pledge reaches the relevant threshold (e.g., a donor pledging $5 million over five years enters the Guardians' Circle upon execution of the pledge and payment of the first $1 million instalment). The naming opportunities (if any) are reserved for the donor upon execution of the pledge and are activated when the pledge is fulfilled. Donors who default on a pledge without renegotiation may have their recognitions suspended, though the university's practice is to work with donors in financial difficulty to find alternative arrangements rather than to revoke recognitions already delivered.</p>
   493→
   494→  <p class="body-text">A final note on anonymity. Donors who wish to remain anonymous may do so at any tier, with the recognition package adapted accordingly. Anonymous donors are recorded in the university's internal records by name but are publicly identified only by a coded entry (e.g., "A Founders' Circle Donor") on the Donor Roll and in public recognitions. The university's staff are bound by confidentiality obligations with respect to anonymous donors' identities, and the donor's anonymity is preserved in perpetuity unless the donor subsequently authorises disclosure. Anonymous donors receive all of the substantive recognitions (Board seat, named entities, etc.) appropriate to their tier; only the public attribution is withheld. Donors considering anonymity should discuss the practical implications with the Office of Gift Planning before finalising their commitment.</p>
