   221→<p class="lead">The founding campaign of the University of Artemis closes by placing donor names on permanent assets &mdash; Colleges, Central Nodes, professorships, centers of inquiry, degree programs, and scholarships. Every naming is recorded as a permanent asset of the institution, governed by a binding gift agreement, and audited annually. This booklet is the complete catalog of the eight naming tiers.</p>
   222→
   223→<p class="body-text">Naming at Artemis is not a marketing gesture. It is a legal and financial instrument by which a donor converts capital into a permanent, named, audited component of the university's balance sheet. The donor's name attaches to an asset &mdash; a building, a faculty position, a research center, a degree, a scholarship &mdash; and the asset is governed under the institution's bylaws, monitored by the Board of Visitors, and reported on the annual audited financial statements. The naming survives the donor, the founders, and the century in which it was made.</p>
   224→
   225→<p class="body-text">This booklet describes each of the eight naming opportunities in detail. For each tier, it states the gift amount, the scope of what is named, the duration of the naming right, the financial structure (endowment versus acquisition), the reporting cadence the donor will receive, and the legal recourse preserved to the donor's estate. It also explains the endowment mathematics that govern every recurring-cost naming &mdash; the 4.5% yield assumption that converts a one-time gift into a perpetual annual cash flow.</p>

   246→<!-- ─── I. THE ARCHITECTURE OF NAMING ─── -->
   247→<div class="chapter-header">
   248→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter I</div>
   249→  <div class="chapter-title">The architecture of naming.</div>
   250→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   251→</div>
   252→
   253→<p class="lead">Naming at Artemis is structured around two distinct financial instruments: the property acquisition and the endowment. A College or Central Node naming funds the acquisition of a physical asset that the institution owns in perpetuity. A professorship, center, degree program, or scholarship naming funds an endowment whose yield covers a recurring cost in perpetuity. Understanding the difference is the prerequisite to understanding what each naming tier buys.</p>
   254→
   255→<p class="body-text">A property naming &mdash; Tier C, Tier B, Tier A, or Central Node &mdash; converts the donor's gift into the acquisition of a building. The building is selected from existing stock (a repurposed convent, warehouse, factory, or office complex) and acquired in fee simple. The donor's name attaches to the building, the College housed within it, and every graduate who passes through it for as long as the institution owns the property. Because the property is owned rather than leased, the naming right is coextensive with the asset &mdash; the name endures as long as the building does, which under Artemis's repurposing philosophy means centuries rather than decades.</p>
   256→
   257→<p class="body-text">An endowment naming &mdash; Distinguished Professorship, Center of Inquiry, Degree Program, or Scholarship Fund &mdash; converts the donor's gift into a restricted corpus whose annual yield covers a specific recurring cost. The corpus is invested under the institution's investment policy, governed by the Board of Visitors, and reported on the annual audit. At a 4.5% spending rate, a $5M endowment generates $225,000 per year &mdash; enough to fund one senior faculty position in perpetuity. A $10M endowment generates $450,000 per year &mdash; enough to operate a research center. A $3M endowment generates $135,000 per year &mdash; enough to underwrite a degree program. A $12K scholarship funds one student for four years at $3,000 per year.</p>
   258→
   259→<div class="callout">
   260→  <div class="callout-label">The two naming instruments</div>
   261→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;"><strong>Property naming</strong> (Tiers C/B/A College + Central Node): the gift acquires a building. The donor's name attaches to the building and the College it houses, in perpetuity. The asset is owned; the name is permanent.<br><br><strong>Endowment naming</strong> (Professorship + Center + Degree + Scholarship): the gift establishes a restricted corpus. The corpus yields 4.5% per year in perpetuity. The donor's name attaches to the recurring cost &mdash; a position, a center, a program, a life &mdash; that the yield covers, forever.</p>
   262→</div>
   263→
   264→<p class="body-text">The eight tiers described in this booklet cover every scale of giving, from the $12K scholarship that funds a single student's four-year journey to the $25M Central Node that anchors an entire continent in the planetary network. Every tier is open to every donor. Every tier is structured to close the founding campaign. Every tier is governed by the same accountability architecture &mdash; a binding gift agreement, an annual audit, a permanent seat on the Board of Visitors (for lead namings), and the legal recourse preserved to the donor's estate if the institution deviates from the terms of the gift.</p>

   280→  <div class="nc-math"><strong>Structure:</strong> property acquisition. The $2M covers building purchase, renovation, and fit-out for a ~1,100-student College in an emerging-knowledge city. <strong>Asset class:</strong> real estate, owned by the institution. <strong>Yield:</strong> the building itself eliminates a recurring lease cost, producing an implicit annual yield equal to the local market rent on a comparable property.</div>
   281→</div>
   282→
   283→<p class="lead">Tier C Colleges are the regional access layer of the planetary network &mdash; twenty-three Colleges placed in the cities the existing university system has failed to serve. A Tier C naming places the donor's name on one of these Colleges, converting a $2M gift into the acquisition of a building that will house generations of students who would otherwise have no access to a frontier curriculum.</p>
   284→
   285→<p class="body-text">The twenty-three Tier C cities are selected by a single criterion: the presence of qualified students whose access to a frontier curriculum is currently blocked by geography, cost, or both. Kigali, Dhaka, Kampala, Karachi, Tashkent, Quito, Accra, Addis Ababa, Lomé, Kathmandu, Colombo, Bishkek, Antananarivo, Managua, San José, Tegucigalpa, La Paz, Ulaanbaatar, Vientiane, Phnom Penh, Maputo, Windhoek, and Harare represent the first wave. Each city contains a population of university-age students large enough to fill a 1,100-student College several times over, and a lack of any existing institution capable of delivering the Artemis curriculum at $3,000 tuition.</p>
   286→
   287→<p class="body-text">The economics of a Tier C naming are deliberately calibrated to the donor who wants to make a regional, place-based commitment. The $2M gift covers the full acquisition cost of a suitable building &mdash; typically a repurposed convent, school, factory, or office complex in the city center, selected for proximity to public transit, walkability, and integration with the surrounding community. The building is acquired in fee simple by the university, renovated to Artemis specifications (tutorial rooms, lecture halls, library, dining, student commons), and operated as a College of Artemis in perpetuity. The donor's name is attached to the building, the College, and every graduate who passes through it.</p>
   288→
   289→<p class="body-text">The donor of a Tier C College receives a permanent seat on the Board of Visitors, an annual audited report on the College's operations, enrollment, and finances, and the right to nominate one student per year for a named scholarship at the College. The naming right is coextensive with the building &mdash; the donor's name remains on the College for as long as the building is owned by the university, which under Artemis's repurposing philosophy means centuries. The gift agreement specifies the named asset, the reporting cadence, the audit cycle, and the legal recourse available to the donor's estate.</p>

   505→<p class="lead">The Degree Program naming is the founding campaign's endowment for the curriculum. Each $3M gift establishes a restricted corpus whose 4.5% annual yield funds one of the institution's 55 degree programs in perpetuity. The donor's name attaches to the degree, every graduate who earns it, and the discipline the degree represents.</p>
   506→
   507→<p class="body-text">The institution's 55 degree programs span nine degree families: Computer Science, International Business, Environmental Science, Philosophy Politics and Economics, Biotechnology, Architecture and Urban Design, Data Science and Statistics, Global History, and Cognitive Science. Within each family, multiple degree variants are offered &mdash; concentrations, joint degrees, dual-location degrees that combine coursework across two or more of the planetary nodes. The 55 named programs are the substantive curriculum of the planetary university, and each one is available for naming by a donor whose commitment is to the discipline it represents.</p>
   508→
   509→<p class="body-text">The endowment mathematics of a Degree Program naming operate at the 4.5% spending rate, producing $135,000 per year from a $3M corpus. The annual yield funds the program's recurring curricular costs &mdash; the curriculum refresh cycle, visiting lecturer honoraria, capstone and thesis prizes, alumni network coordination, and the program's standing annual convocation. The program's faculty compensation is covered by the operating P&amp;L; the endowment covers the program's identity, community, and intellectual life. The distinction matters: the endowment does not fund operations, it funds permanence.</p>

After Year 1, the operating surplus funds 13,300 scholarships per year, but the founding cohort of 10,000 scholarship students must be funded by donor capital. A $12K Scholarship Fund covers one of those 10,000 students for the full four years.</p>

   570→<!-- ─── X. THE ENDOWMENT MATH ─── -->
   571→<div class="chapter-header">
   572→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter X</div>
   573→  <div class="chapter-title">The endowment math &mdash; 4.5% forever.</div>
   574→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   575→</div>
   576→
   577→<p class="lead">Every endowment naming in the founding campaign &mdash; Distinguished Professorship, Center of Inquiry, Degree Program &mdash; is governed by the same spending-rate architecture: a 4.5% annual distribution from a restricted corpus invested under the institution's investment policy. This chapter explains the math, the assumptions, the reporting, and the recourse that together make the endowment naming a permanent, audited, legally enforceable instrument.</p>
   578→
   579→<p class="body-text">The 4.5% spending rate is the product of three deliberate choices. First, the institution targets a long-term net investment return of 6.5% on the endowment corpus &mdash; a conservative assumption based on a diversified portfolio of global equities, fixed income, real estate, and alternative assets, calibrated to the long-term returns achieved by mature university endowments over the past half-century. Second, the institution retains approximately 2% of net return in the corpus each year to offset inflation, ensuring that the spending distribution maintains its real purchasing power across decades. Third, the institution distributes 4.5% of corpus value annually to the named purpose &mdash; the position, the center, or the program &mdash; producing a predictable, durable, perpetual cash flow.</p>
   580→
   581→<p class="body-text">The math is straightforward. A $5M corpus yields $225,000 in Year 1. A $10M corpus yields $450,000. A $3M corpus yields $135,000. The spending rate is applied to a rolling average of corpus value over the prior twelve quarters, smoothing the distribution against short-term market volatility. The corpus compounds at the retained 2% real rate, meaning that a $5M corpus becomes approximately $6.5M after ten years, $13.5M after fifty years, and $36M after a century &mdash; at which point the 4.5% spending rate produces $1.6M per year from the original $5M gift. This is the architecture by which a single endowment naming compounds into a century of permanent, growing, audited cash flow.</p>

   601→<p class="body-text">The endowment is governed by the institution's investment policy &mdash; a publicly available document specifying the asset allocation, the rebalancing rules, the spending-rate formula, and the audit cycle. The corpus is held in a restricted account segregated from operating funds; the spending distribution is transferred to the operating account annually and applied to the named purpose. The endowment is reported on the institution's annual audited financial statements, with the named corpus identified separately and its spending distribution disclosed. The donor receives an annual audited statement of the corpus value, the investment return, the spending distribution, and the named purpose's activities.</p>
   602→
   603→<div class="callout">
   604→  <div class="callout-label">Legal recourse and the binding gift agreement</div>
   605→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">Every endowment naming is governed by a binding gift agreement that specifies the named purpose, the spending rate, the reporting cadence, the audit cycle, and the legal recourse available to the donor's estate if the institution deviates from the terms. The gift agreement is enforceable under Delaware Non-Stock Corporation law and is registered with the institution's legal counsel. If the institution fails to apply the spending distribution to the named purpose, or fails to maintain the corpus at the required minimum, the donor's estate (or its designated successor) has standing to compel performance through the Delaware Court of Chancery.</p>
   606→</div>
   607→
   608→<p class="body-text">The endowment architecture is the mechanism by which a single gift becomes permanent. The donor who gives $5M to name a Distinguished Professorship is not giving $5M to a year of operations &mdash; they are giving $5M to a century of positions, each funded by the yield on a corpus that compounds and survives. The donor who gives $10M to name a Center of Inquiry is not giving $10M to a research project &mdash; they are giving $10M to a century of research, embodied in the succession of investigators and fellows who occupy the Center across decades. The architecture is the institution's commitment that a naming gift is not a contribution to a budget line but a permanent conversion of capital into a recurring purpose.</p>

   610→<div class="pullquote">
   611→  &ldquo;The 4.5% spending rate is the institution's promise to every endowment donor: your gift will fund the named purpose this year, next year, in the year your grandchildren retire, and in the year the institution celebrates its centenary. The corpus will be larger then. The purpose will be the same.&rdquo;

   616→<div class="chapter-header">
   617→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter XI</div>
   618→  <div class="chapter-title">How to secure a naming.</div>
   619→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   620→</div>
   621→
   622→<p class="lead">Securing a naming is a structured process: an expression of interest, a due-diligence exchange, a binding gift agreement, and a pledge schedule. This chapter describes each step and the timeline within which a naming is committed, executed, and recognized.</p>
   623→
   624→<p class="body-text">The process begins with an expression of interest &mdash; an email, a call, or a meeting with the founding team that identifies the naming tier, the geographic or disciplinary preference, and the donor's timeline. The founding team responds within five business days with a tailored proposal specifying the named asset, the gift amount, the gift agreement terms, the reporting cadence, and the recognition the donor will receive. The proposal includes the institution's audited financial statements, the bylaws, the gift agreement template, and the legal opinion on the donor's home-jurisdiction tax treatment.</p>
   625→
   626→<p class="body-text">The donor conducts due diligence &mdash; a review of the financial model, the campus master plan, the academic prospectus, the legal entities overview, and the accountability architecture. The institution's legal counsel is available to the donor's counsel for any clarifying exchange. The due-diligence process typically takes two to six weeks, depending on the donor's existing familiarity with the institution and the complexity of the donor's tax structure. The institution does not impose a deadline on due diligence; the founding window is open, but each naming is held for the donor until the donor confirms or withdraws.</p>

Pledges may be paid over three to five years for namings above $1M, with the named asset acquired or established when the pledge is sufficiently advanced to support the underlying capital expenditure. The donor may structure the gift through a direct gift agreement with the institution, through a fiscal sponsor (Rockefeller Philanthropy Advisors, NGOsource, or Global Impact), or through a donor-advised fund. Securities, real estate, and cryptocurrency are accepted at fair market value.</p>

   649→  <div class="callout-label">The closing window</div>
   650→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">The founding campaign is engineered to close, not to remain perpetually open. The quiet phase secures lead commitments &mdash; the three Central Nodes, the eight Tier A Colleges, the named Distinguished Professorships and Centers of Inquiry &mdash; and once the quiet phase reaches its $50M threshold, the campaign moves to public phase with the conviction that the lead segment is committed and the major and community segments will follow. The donor who waits for the public phase to begin before committing will give into a campaign that is already half closed; the donor who commits in the quiet phase gives into the campaign that determines whether the institution is built at all.</p>

Every tier is open. Every tier is governed by the same accountability architecture. Every tier is recorded as a permanent asset of the founding. The donor who secures a naming in the next nine months acquires a permanent association with the planetary university &mdash; an association that will outlive them, the founders, and the century in which the gift was made.</p>
