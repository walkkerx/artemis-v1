   698→  <div class="chapter-title">How Founding Campaigns Work</div>
   699→</div>
   700→<p class="lead">Founding campaigns do not raise money from crowds. They raise money from a small number of people who understand that institutions are built before they are announced. The Artemis campaign follows the standard distribution: 70% from 10 donors, 20% from 20 donors, 10% from hundreds.</p>
   701→<p class="body-text">This distribution is not a preference — it is a structural fact about how institutions are founded. Yale's "For Humanity" campaign secured $3.5 billion in its quiet phase, before any public announcement, from fewer than fifty donors. Stanford's campaign raised more than 70% of its goal from lead donors before the public launch. The pattern is consistent because the logic is consistent: lead donors commit privately because they understand that their commitment is what makes the public campaign credible. Without the lead gifts, there is nothing to announce.</p>

The secondary vehicle, in England &amp; Wales, is a Charitable Incorporated Organisation eligible for Gift Aid. The tertiary vehicle, planned for Switzerland, is a Fondation under the Swiss Civil Code, providing endowment management in a neutral jurisdiction.</p>

   902→<p class="lead">We are asking for one hundred million dollars from approximately thirty donors to build a university that will, within a decade, generate a five-hundred-and-forty-three-million-dollar endowment and educate sixty thousand students per year. We are asking you to be one of those thirty.</p>
   903→<p class="body-text">The ask is precise. The campaign requires ten lead gifts at an average of $7M each, and twenty major gifts at an average of $1M each. Every lead gift is structured around a named asset — a Central Node, a College, a Center of Inquiry, or a Distinguished Professorship — that becomes a permanent part of the institution's physical or academic architecture. Every major gift funds a Professorship, a Degree Program naming, or a Scholarship Fund that becomes a permanent line in the university's endowment.</p>
   904→<p class="body-text">The minimum founding gift that secures a named asset is $12,000 — a four-year scholarship for one student. The maximum is $25M — the naming of a Central Node in Venice, San Francisco, or Singapore. Between these two figures there are seven distinct naming opportunities, each with its own financial structure, its own recognition, and its own place in the founding architecture. There is a path for every serious founding donor.</p>
