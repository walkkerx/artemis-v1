   215→
   216→<p class="lead">The University of Artemis operates across fifty Colleges in thirty-five countries, anchored by three Central Nodes in Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore. No Artemis campus is built from scratch. Every building is acquired from existing stock &mdash; repurposed convents, warehouses, factories, and offices &mdash; and owned in fee simple by the institution. The campus master plan is the strategy by which this planetary network is assembled, financed, and operated.</p>
   217→
   218→<p class="body-text">The repurposing philosophy is the single most important decision in the campus plan, because it determines the economics of the entire institution. A traditional research university spends between $200M and $1B to build a flagship campus; Artemis acquires a comparable facility for $5M&ndash;$25M by repurposing existing buildings that already exist in the cities the institution wishes to occupy. The savings &mdash; on the order of $500M per campus &mdash; are what make the $100M founding campaign sufficient to launch a planetary university rather than a single regional one.</p>
   219→
   220→<p class="body-text">The fifty Colleges are organized in three tiers: eight Tier A flagship Colleges in the world's knowledge capitals (London, Tokyo, Berlin, Paris, Toronto, Sydney, Seoul, New York); sixteen Tier B Colleges in major global cities across all six continents (Nairobi, Mumbai, São Paulo, Cairo, Istanbul, Buenos Aires, Jakarta, Mexico City, and more); and twenty-three Tier C regional access Colleges in cities the existing university system has failed to serve (Kigali, Dhaka, Kampala, Karachi, and beyond). The three Central Nodes are the apex &mdash; Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore &mdash; each housing approximately 5,000 students and serving as the regional seat of governance, convocation, and the founding document archive.</p>

   241→<!-- ─── I. THE REPURPOSING PHILOSOPHY ─── -->
   242→<div class="chapter-header">
   243→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter I</div>
   244→  <div class="chapter-title">The repurposing philosophy &mdash; why we do not build.</div>
   245→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   246→</div>
   247→
   248→<p class="lead">The single most important decision in the Artemis campus plan is the decision not to build. Every Artemis College is housed in a repurposed building &mdash; a convent, a warehouse, a factory, an office complex &mdash; acquired from existing stock, owned in fee simple, and adapted to the institution's specifications. This decision is the foundation of the financial model, the sustainability model, and the timeline by which the planetary university is launched.</p>
   249→
   250→<p class="body-text">The decision rests on three reasons. The first is environmental. New construction is among the most carbon-intensive activities in the modern economy: a single new academic building typically embodies between 500 and 1,500 tonnes of CO2 in its concrete, steel, and glass, and that embodied carbon is emitted before the building is ever occupied. Repurposing an existing building saves between 60% and 80% of the embodied carbon that would be expended in new construction &mdash; a saving that compounds across fifty Colleges into the carbon equivalent of a small city's annual footprint. The planetary university cannot claim to address the twenty-first century's challenges while expending the twentieth century's construction carbon to build itself.</p>
   251→
   252→<p class="body-text">The second reason is cost. A new flagship academic building in a Tier-1 global city costs between $50M and $200M, depending on land acquisition, design fees, and construction complexity. A comparable repurposed building &mdash; with equivalent floor area, equivalent occupancy, and equivalent operational capacity &mdash; costs between $5M and $25M. The savings, multiplied across fifty Colleges, are what permit the $100M founding campaign to launch a planetary university rather than a single regional one. The campus plan is, in this sense, a financial instrument: the decision not to build is the decision to make the planetary model mathematically possible.</p>
   253→
   254→<div class="figure">
   255→  <img src="../public/resources/img/campus-architecture.png" alt="Repurposed campus building">
   256→  <div class="caption">A repurposed College &mdash; acquired from existing stock, owned in fee simple, adapted to the institution's specifications. The decision not to build is what makes the planetary university possible.</div>
   257→</div>
   258→
   259→<p class="body-text">The third reason is speed. A new academic building takes between three and seven years from architectural design through permitting, construction, and occupancy &mdash; a timeline that would push the planetary university's launch past the closing window of the founding campaign. A repurposed building takes between six and eighteen months from acquisition through renovation to occupancy &mdash; a timeline that permits the institution to be operational within the campaign's twelve-month horizon. The campus plan is therefore also a calendar: repurposing is the only path that allows the founding campaign to close and the university to open within the same year.</p>
   260→
   261→<p class="body-text">A fourth consideration reinforces the first three: the integration of the College with the surrounding city. A repurposed building is, by definition, already embedded in an urban fabric &mdash; with surrounding transit, residential density, commercial amenity, and civic infrastructure. A new building, by contrast, often requires the surrounding fabric to be re-engineered to support it. The repurposing philosophy is therefore also an urbanist philosophy: the College joins the city, the city does not reorganize itself around the College. This is what permits each Artemis College to be a local institution as well as a planetary one &mdash; embedded in the city that hosts it rather than isolated from it.</p>
   262→
   263→<div class="callout">
   264→  <div class="callout-label">The repurposing calculus</div>
   265→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">A repurposed College in a Tier-1 city costs $5M&ndash;$25M. A new-build College in the same city costs $50M&ndash;$200M. The savings compound across fifty Colleges into $1B&ndash;$5B &mdash; the difference between a planetary university and a regional one, and the difference between a $100M founding campaign and a $5B one. The repurposing philosophy is what makes the planetary model financially possible.</p>
   266→</div>
   267→
   268→<p class="body-text">The repurposing philosophy also shapes the institution's relationship with its buildings over time. Because each building is owned rather than leased, the institution can renovate, expand, or repurpose the building further as the College grows &mdash; without negotiating with a landlord, without rent escalation, and without the displacement risk that lease-dependent institutions face. The buildings become permanent assets of the institution's balance sheet, appreciating in value over decades, and providing collateral capacity that supports the institution's long-term financing. The campus plan, in this sense, is also a balance-sheet strategy: the property portfolio is the institutional asset base.</p>
   269→
   270→<!-- ─── II. PROPERTY ACQUISITION STRATEGY ─── -->
   271→<div class="chapter-header">
   272→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter II</div>
   273→  <div class="chapter-title">Property acquisition strategy.</div>
   274→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   275→</div>
   276→
   277→<p class="lead">The acquisition of fifty College buildings and three Central Node campuses across thirty-five countries is the largest single line item in the founding campaign &mdash; $82M of the $100M goal, allocated to the Place pillar. The acquisition strategy specifies how buildings are sourced, evaluated, acquired, and integrated into the planetary network.</p>
   278→
   279→<p class="body-text">Building sourcing begins twelve to eighteen months before a College is scheduled to open. The founding team identifies candidate cities based on the tier framework (Tier A, B, or C), then surveys the city's existing building stock for properties that meet four criteria: adequate floor area (typically 8,000&ndash;25,000 square meters, depending on tier); appropriate floorplates for tutorial rooms, lecture halls, library, and commons; central location with transit access; and a building condition that permits renovation within the budget and timeline constraints. Candidate buildings include repurposed convents and monasteries (often vacant due to declining religious orders), warehouses and factories (vacated by industrial relocation), office complexes (underutilized due to remote-work shifts), and former schools or government buildings (decommissioned due to demographic change).</p>
   280→
   281→<p class="body-text">Each candidate building is evaluated against a standard assessment template covering structural condition, environmental performance, regulatory status, acquisition cost, renovation cost, and operating cost. The assessment is conducted by a local architect and a local surveyor, with the founding team providing the institutional specification. Buildings that pass the assessment enter the acquisition pipeline; buildings that fail are recorded for future reference. The pipeline is maintained at three to five candidate buildings per planned College, ensuring that the institution can proceed with an alternative if the preferred building is withdrawn or if the acquisition cost exceeds the budget envelope.</p>

   300→<p class="body-text">Acquisition is structured as a fee-simple purchase wherever local law permits, which is the case in forty-two of the fifty planned College cities and all three Central Node cities. In the eight cities where foreign-institution ownership is restricted, the institution acquires the building through a long-term lease (typically 99 years) with a local foundation partner that holds legal title in trust for the university. The lease structure includes the institution's right to renovate, operate, and rename the building, with the underlying title reverting to the institution if and when local law permits foreign ownership. In every case, the institution's operating control over the building is absolute; only the legal form of the title varies.</p>

   501→  <div class="chapter-title">Sustainability &mdash; embodied and operational carbon.</div>
   502→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   503→</div>
   504→
   505→<p class="lead">The repurposing philosophy is the institution's primary climate instrument. New academic construction is among the most carbon-intensive activities in the modern economy, and a planetary university built from scratch would expend the carbon of a small city before admitting a single student. By repurposing fifty-three existing buildings, the institution avoids between 60% and 80% of the embodied carbon that new construction would require, while operating the network at a fraction of the per-student energy footprint of the traditional research university.</p>
   506→
   507→<p class="body-text">The carbon case for repurposing is mathematical. A new academic building of 15,000 square meters &mdash; the typical Tier A College specification &mdash; embodies between 4,500 and 13,500 tonnes of CO2 in its concrete, steel, glass, and finishes. A repurposed building of equivalent floor area, by contrast, typically requires only 1,000 to 3,000 tonnes of CO2 in renovation materials, mechanical systems, and finishes. Across fifty-three campuses, the saving is between 175,000 and 555,000 tonnes of CO2 &mdash; the annual carbon footprint of a city of 30,000 people. The planetary university cannot claim to address the twenty-first century's challenges while expending the twentieth century's construction carbon to build itself.</p>
   508→
   509→<p class="body-text">The operational carbon case is equally compelling. New academic buildings typically consume between 250 and 400 kilowatt-hours per square meter per year, driven by deep floorplates, mechanical ventilation, and intensive cooling loads. Repurposed buildings, with shallower floorplates and natural cross-ventilation possible through original window arrangements, typically consume between 120 and 200 kWh per square meter per year &mdash; a 40% to 50% reduction in operational energy. Across the 600,000-square-meter planetary portfolio, the operational saving is on the order of 60 to 100 gigawatt-hours per year, the consumption of a small city.</p>

   529→
   530→<p class="body-text">The renovation specification reinforces the carbon strategy. Insulation is upgraded to modern standards where original construction permits, glazing is replaced with high-performance units where heritage regulations allow, and mechanical systems are specified to district-heating or heat-pump configurations where local infrastructure supports them. Each renovation includes a building-management system that monitors consumption in real time, with the data aggregated across the network and reported in the annual sustainability audit. The planetary portfolio's energy intensity is therefore a measurable, reportable, and improvable quantity &mdash; not a vague aspiration but a tracked metric with annual targets.</p>
   531→
   532→<p class="body-text">The institution's century-scale design horizon is itself a sustainability strategy. A building renovated to institutional specification and operated for a century amortizes its embodied carbon over a far longer period than the typical commercial building, which is replaced every 30 to 50 years. The Artemis buildings are designed to outlive the founders, the founding cohort, and the century in which they were acquired. The carbon expended in their renovation is therefore an investment in a multi-generational asset, not a one-time expense &mdash; the only honest way to count the carbon of an institution that intends to be permanent.</p>
   533→
   534→<div class="callout">
   535→  <div class="callout-label">The annual sustainability audit</div>
   536→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">Each Artemis building reports embodied carbon (one-time, at renovation), operational energy (annual, by source), water consumption, waste diversion, and indoor environmental quality. The data is consolidated into the institutional Sustainability Report, audited alongside the financial statements, and published annually. Donors who name a building receive the sustainability audit for their named asset in perpetuity &mdash; the carbon accounting of a permanent gift.</p>

   539→<!-- ─── X. THE FOUNDING TIMELINE ─── -->
   540→<div class="chapter-header">
   541→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter X</div>
   542→  <div class="chapter-title">The founding timeline &mdash; from campaign close to Day 1.</div>
   543→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   544→</div>
   545→
   546→<p class="lead">The founding campaign closes on a fixed date, and the university opens on a fixed date twelve months later. The compression of these two events into a single calendar year is the operational consequence of the repurposing philosophy: because the buildings already exist, the institution can move from campaign close to Day 1 operations in the time a new-build university would still be in design review.</p>
   547→
   548→<p class="body-text">The twelve-month timeline decomposes into four overlapping quarters. In the first quarter, the founding team confirms the founding gift agreements, executes the property acquisitions for the three Central Nodes and the first twelve Colleges, and recruits the founding faculty cohort of 200. The first quarter is also when the institutional treasury is capitalized, the legal entities across the three jurisdictions are activated, and the digital infrastructure contract is signed. By the end of the first quarter, the institution has its assets, its faculty pipeline, and its operating infrastructure in place.</p>
   549→
   550→<p class="body-text">In the second quarter, renovation begins on the three Central Nodes and the first twelve Colleges, with the founding team conducting the institutional specification process and supervising local contractors. The founding faculty cohort is fully recruited (200 of the 2,000 Year-1 faculty), and the admissions process opens for the founding student cohort of 120. The second quarter is also when the curriculum is finalized, the tutorial pairings are designed, and the first lecture series is rehearsed. By the end of the second quarter, the buildings are mid-renovation, the faculty is largely in place, and the admissions decisions are being made.</p>
   551→
   552→<div class="figure">
   553→  <img src="../public/resources/img/research-lab.png" alt="Renovation specification and faculty preparation">
   554→  <div class="caption">The founding-year specification process &mdash; institutional standards for tutorial rooms, lecture halls, library stacks, and hybrid-delivery infrastructure are deployed simultaneously across fifty-three campuses during the second and third quarters of the founding year.</div>
   555→</div>
   556→
   557→<p class="body-text">In the third quarter, renovation completes on the first twelve Colleges, the founding student cohort of 120 is admitted and housed, and the faculty orientation program begins. The digital infrastructure is tested across the network: hybrid-delivery lecture halls are calibrated, tutorial collaboration tools are deployed, and the asynchronous video library is populated with the first lecture series. The third quarter is when the network becomes operational &mdash; not yet teaching students, but capable of doing so. By the end of the third quarter, the first twelve Colleges are physically and digitally ready to open.</p>
   558→
   559→<p class="body-text">In the fourth quarter, the three Central Nodes complete renovation, the founding convocation is held in Venice, and the university opens on Day 1. The first lecture is delivered (in Venice, broadcast across the network), the first tutorials are convened (in every College, simultaneously), and the first cohort of 120 students begins the planetary curriculum. The fourth quarter is also when the second wave of property acquisitions begins &mdash; the remaining 38 Colleges, which will open in Years 2 through 5 as renovation completes. The founding year closes with the university operational, the founding cohort matriculated, and the second wave of expansion underway.</p>
   560→
   561→<div class="callout">
   562→  <div class="callout-label">Why twelve months, not seven years</div>
   563→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">A traditional research university takes between five and ten years from gift to opening &mdash; the design, permitting, and construction timeline of a new campus. Artemis takes twelve months because the buildings already exist. The twelve-month timeline is the operational expression of the repurposing philosophy: the campus plan is a calendar as much as it is a financial instrument or a sustainability strategy.</p>
   564→</div>
   565→
   566→<p class="body-text">The twelve-month timeline is also a fundraising instrument. Donors who give to the founding campaign see their gifts converted into operational reality within the same calendar year &mdash; a named College in March, an open College by the following March. The compression of gift-to-impact is rare in higher-education philanthropy, where the typical lag between a naming gift and the building's completion is three to seven years. The Artemis donor is therefore participating in an unusually legible form of philanthropy: the gift produces a permanent, named, operational asset, within a single fiscal cycle.</p>
   567→
   568→<!-- ─── XI. OPERATIONS IN PRACTICE ─── -->
   569→<div class="chapter-header">
   570→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter XI</div>
   571→  <div class="chapter-title">Operations in practice &mdash; faculty, convocation, and the single treasury.</div>
   572→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   573→</div>
   574→
   575→<p class="lead">A planetary network of fifty-three campuses is, in operational terms, an exercise in shared infrastructure. The curriculum is shared, the faculty roster is shared, the treasury is shared, and the calendar of convocations is shared. The campus plan therefore specifies not only what is built but how the buildings are operated together &mdash; the operational architecture that converts fifty-three physical locations into a single institution.</p>
   576→
   577→<p class="body-text">The faculty roster is the most operationally complex shared infrastructure. The 2,000-faculty roster at maturity is recruited, compensated, and rotated centrally &mdash; with each faculty member holding a home College and rotating through the network for at least one term every three years. A professor based in Venice may teach a tutorial in Singapore in the fall, a lecture in Berlin in the spring, and a research sabbatical in San Francisco in the summer. The rotation ensures that every College, regardless of tier, has access to the institution's full faculty &mdash; the structural condition for the network's claim that a graduate of the Kampala College holds the same degree as a graduate of the London College.</p>
   578→
   579→<p class="body-text">Faculty compensation is set centrally, on a single global salary scale calibrated to the local cost of living in each College city. The single scale prevents the salary arbitrage that would otherwise hollow out Tier C Colleges (where local market rates are low) or inflate Tier A Colleges (where local market rates are high). A professor in Nairobi is paid on the same scale as a professor in New York, adjusted for housing and tax differentials, ensuring that the network's faculty is recruited on the basis of intellectual fit rather than compensation differential. The single scale is also the institution's defense against the brain drain that has historically disadvantaged universities in lower-income countries.</p>
   580→
   581→<div class="figure">
   582→  <img src="../public/resources/img/students-global.png" alt="Students collaborating across the planetary network">
   583→  <div class="caption">The student experience of the planetary network &mdash; every Artemis student is entitled to spend at least one academic term at a node other than their home College, with tuition, credit, and financial aid flowing seamlessly across the network.</div>
   584→</div>
   585→
   586→<p class="body-text">The convocation calendar is the rhythm of the academic year. Each Central Node hosts one annual founding convocation: Venice in October (the founding seat, where new graduates are sworn in), San Francisco in February (the innovation seat, where the Forge portfolio is reviewed), and Singapore in June (the Asia-Pacific seat, where the regional gatherings convene). The three convocations are open to every student and faculty member, with travel funded from the operating P&amp;L for the founding cohort. The convocation calendar is the mechanism by which the planetary network acquires its institutional identity &mdash; the rhythm by which a network of fifty-three campuses becomes a single university.</p>
   587→
   588→<p class="body-text">The single treasury is the financial connective tissue. Tuition, endowment payout, research grants, Forge equity distributions, and continuing-education revenue flow into a single institutional account, from which faculty compensation, facilities operations, scholarships, research, and administration are paid. The single treasury is what permits the operating model to produce the $262M annual surplus from Year 2 onward, and what permits the institution to charge $3,000 tuition while the Ivy League charges $60,000. A federated treasury, with each College managing its own finances, would replicate the cost structure of the existing university system; the single treasury is what makes the planetary model financially distinctive.</p>
   589→
   590→<div class="callout">
   591→  <div class="callout-label">The single salary, single treasury, single curriculum</div>
   592→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">Three architectural decisions make the planetary university possible: a single faculty salary scale (no arbitrage), a single institutional treasury (no federation), and a single shared curriculum (no local variation in degree). Together, these decisions are what convert fifty-three buildings into a single university &mdash; and what distinguish the planetary model from a federation of independent institutions.</p>
   593→</div>
   594→
   595→<p class="body-text">The operational infrastructure is supported by an administrative staff of approximately 200 FTE at maturity &mdash; 3% of the operating budget, against the 15% to 25% administrative overhead typical of traditional research universities. The administrative compression is possible because the network shares its administrative functions: a single admissions office, a single registrar, a single financial-aid office, a single facilities team, a single IT team, a single development office. The administrative overhead is the operational expression of the network's compounding advantage: marginal students and marginal faculty are added at marginal administrative cost, because the shared infrastructure already exists.</p>

   597→<!-- ─── XII. THE CENTURY AHEAD ─── -->
   598→<div class="chapter-header">
   599→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter XII</div>
   600→  <div class="chapter-title">The century ahead &mdash; expansion beyond Year 1.</div>
   601→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   602→</div>
   603→
   604→<p class="lead">The fifty-three campuses of Year 1 are not the final configuration of the planetary university &mdash; they are the founding configuration. The institution's design horizon extends to a century and beyond, with the network architecture permitting expansion at marginal cost as new cities are added, new research centers are endowed, and new generations of students are admitted. The campus plan is therefore also a century-scale expansion plan: the founding architecture is the seed of an institution that grows over the century that follows its founding.</p>
   605→
   606→<p class="body-text">The expansion path is built into the network architecture. A new College in a city not yet on the map requires acquisition and renovation ($2M&ndash;$25M, depending on tier), connection to the shared digital infrastructure, and integration into the faculty rotation &mdash; but does not require additional curriculum development, additional administrative overhead, or additional central infrastructure. The marginal cost of adding the fifty-fourth College is therefore on the order of $5M, against the average cost of $1.6M per College in the founding fifty. The network's compounding advantage is that each new College becomes cheaper to add than the one before, because the shared infrastructure is already in place.</p>
   607→
   608→<p class="body-text">The strategic expansion priorities for the second decade are the cities the founding fifty does not reach: second-tier cities in sub-Saharan Africa (Accra, Addis Ababa, Dakar, Dar es Salaam), the secondary capitals of South Asia (Colombo, Kathmandu), and the emerging knowledge cities of Latin America (Medellín, Curitiba, Monterrey). These cities are the access mission's second wave &mdash; the places where the planetary university's $3,000 tuition and need-blind admission will have the largest marginal impact on the access pipeline. The expansion is sequenced to maintain the founding commitment: governance first, then flagship presence, then regional capital, then access &mdash; with the second decade weighted toward access.</p>
   609→
   610→<p class="body-text">The research infrastructure expands in parallel. The nineteen Centers of Inquiry of Year 1 are the founding configuration of a research portfolio that is expected to grow to forty or fifty centers by Year 25, as new endowments are added and new research domains emerge. Each new Center is endowed at $10M (the Center of Inquiry naming tier), with the endowment generating $450K per year in perpetuity to fund the center's research program. The expansion of the research portfolio is therefore self-funding &mdash; each new center is capitalized by a donor gift, with the operating P&amp;L covering the marginal cost of integration into the existing research infrastructure.</p>
   611→
   612→<div class="figure">
   613→  <img src="../public/resources/img/governance-building.png" alt="Governance architecture of the planetary university">
   614→  <div class="caption">The governance architecture &mdash; the Board of Visitors, the Academic Council, and the Founders' Council are the institutional structures that govern the planetary university across the century ahead, with the founding document archived at Venice as the constitutional anchor.</div>
   615→</div>
   616→
   617→<p class="body-text">The century-scale design horizon also shapes the institution's relationship with its buildings. Each Artemis building is owned, not leased, and is therefore maintained to a century-scale specification: roofs are replaced on a 50-year cycle, mechanical systems on a 25-year cycle, finishes on a 10-year cycle. The maintenance schedule is funded from the operating P&amp;L, with the facilities budget calibrated to the long-term preservation of the asset rather than the short-term minimization of cost. The buildings appreciate in value over the century, providing collateral capacity that supports the institution's long-term financing &mdash; the campus plan as a balance-sheet strategy, sustained across generations.</p>
   618→
   619→<div class="callout">
   620→  <div class="callout-label">The century metric</div>
   621→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">The institution measures its success on century-scale metrics: the number of buildings still owned and operated, the number of degree programs still taught, the number of graduates who have lived their full careers under the institution's charter. The founding cohort will not see the institution's century. The donors who name its buildings will not see it. The campus plan is designed for the generations that will &mdash; the students of 2125, the faculty of 2075, the graduates whose careers the planetary university will anchor across the entire twenty-first century.</p>
   622→</div>
   623→
   624→<p class="body-text">The campus plan closes with the institutional commitment that motivates the century-scale design horizon. The planetary university is founded not as a venture with an exit, but as a permanent institution whose architecture, finances, and operations are designed to outlive every founder, every donor, and every century in which it operates. The fifty-three campuses of Year 1 are the seed; the century ahead is the institution's actual horizon. The campus plan is the document by which that horizon is made operational &mdash; repurposed, not built; owned, not leased; permanent.</p>
