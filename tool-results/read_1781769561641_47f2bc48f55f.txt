   214→
   215→<p class="lead">The University of Artemis operates across fifty Colleges in thirty-five countries, anchored by three Central Nodes in Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore. No Artemis campus is built from scratch. Every building is acquired from existing stock &mdash; repurposed convents, warehouses, factories, and offices &mdash; and owned in fee simple by the institution. The campus master plan is the strategy by which this planetary network is assembled, financed, and operated.</p>
   216→
   217→<p class="body-text">The repurposing philosophy is the single most important decision in the campus plan, because it determines the economics of the entire institution. A traditional research university spends between $200M and $1B to build a flagship campus; Artemis acquires a comparable facility for $5M&ndash;$25M by repurposing existing buildings that already exist in the cities the institution wishes to occupy. The savings &mdash; on the order of $500M per campus &mdash; are what make the $100M founding campaign sufficient to launch a planetary university rather than a single regional one.</p>
   218→
   219→<p class="body-text">The fifty Colleges are organized in three tiers: eight Tier A flagship Colleges in the world's knowledge capitals (London, Tokyo, Berlin, Paris, Toronto, Sydney, Seoul, New York); sixteen Tier B Colleges in major global cities across all six continents (Nairobi, Mumbai, São Paulo, Cairo, Istanbul, Buenos Aires, Jakarta, Mexico City, and more); and twenty-three Tier C regional access Colleges in cities the existing university system has failed to serve (Kigali, Dhaka, Kampala, Karachi, and beyond). The three Central Nodes are the apex &mdash; Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore &mdash; each housing approximately 5,000 students and serving as the regional seat of governance, convocation, and the founding document archive.</p>

   240→<!-- ─── I. THE REPURPOSING PHILOSOPHY ─── -->
   241→<div class="chapter-header">
   242→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter I</div>
   243→  <div class="chapter-title">The repurposing philosophy &mdash; why we do not build.</div>
   244→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   245→</div>
   246→
   247→<p class="lead">The single most important decision in the Artemis campus plan is the decision not to build. Every Artemis College is housed in a repurposed building &mdash; a convent, a warehouse, a factory, an office complex &mdash; acquired from existing stock, owned in fee simple, and adapted to the institution's specifications. This decision is the foundation of the financial model, the sustainability model, and the timeline by which the planetary university is launched.</p>
   248→
   249→<p class="body-text">The decision rests on three reasons. The first is environmental. New construction is among the most carbon-intensive activities in the modern economy: a single new academic building typically embodies between 500 and 1,500 tonnes of CO2 in its concrete, steel, and glass, and that embodied carbon is emitted before the building is ever occupied. Repurposing an existing building saves between 60% and 80% of the embodied carbon that would be expended in new construction &mdash; a saving that compounds across fifty Colleges into the carbon equivalent of a small city's annual footprint. The planetary university cannot claim to address the twenty-first century's challenges while expending the twentieth century's construction carbon to build itself.</p>
   250→
   251→<p class="body-text">The second reason is cost. A new flagship academic building in a Tier-1 global city costs between $50M and $200M, depending on land acquisition, design fees, and construction complexity. A comparable repurposed building &mdash; with equivalent floor area, equivalent occupancy, and equivalent operational capacity &mdash; costs between $5M and $25M. The savings, multiplied across fifty Colleges, are what permit the $100M founding campaign to launch a planetary university rather than a single regional one. The campus plan is, in this sense, a financial instrument: the decision not to build is the decision to make the planetary model mathematically possible.</p>
   252→
   253→<div class="figure">
   254→  <img src="../public/resources/img/campus-architecture.png" alt="Repurposed campus building">
   255→  <div class="caption">A repurposed College &mdash; acquired from existing stock, owned in fee simple, adapted to the institution's specifications. The decision not to build is what makes the planetary university possible.</div>
   256→</div>
   257→
   258→<p class="body-text">The third reason is speed. A new academic building takes between three and seven years from architectural design through permitting, construction, and occupancy &mdash; a timeline that would push the planetary university's launch past the closing window of the founding campaign. A repurposed building takes between six and eighteen months from acquisition through renovation to occupancy &mdash; a timeline that permits the institution to be operational within the campaign's twelve-month horizon. The campus plan is therefore also a calendar: repurposing is the only path that allows the founding campaign to close and the university to open within the same year.</p>
   259→
   260→<p class="body-text">A fourth consideration reinforces the first three: the integration of the College with the surrounding city. A repurposed building is, by definition, already embedded in an urban fabric &mdash; with surrounding transit, residential density, commercial amenity, and civic infrastructure. A new building, by contrast, often requires the surrounding fabric to be re-engineered to support it. The repurposing philosophy is therefore also an urbanist philosophy: the College joins the city, the city does not reorganize itself around the College. This is what permits each Artemis College to be a local institution as well as a planetary one &mdash; embedded in the city that hosts it rather than isolated from it.</p>
   261→
   262→<div class="callout">
   263→  <div class="callout-label">The repurposing calculus</div>
   264→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">A repurposed College in a Tier-1 city costs $5M&ndash;$25M. A new-build College in the same city costs $50M&ndash;$200M. The savings compound across fifty Colleges into $1B&ndash;$5B &mdash; the difference between a planetary university and a regional one, and the difference between a $100M founding campaign and a $5B one. The repurposing philosophy is what makes the planetary model financially possible.</p>
   265→</div>
   266→
   267→<p class="body-text">The repurposing philosophy also shapes the institution's relationship with its buildings over time. Because each building is owned rather than leased, the institution can renovate, expand, or repurpose the building further as the College grows &mdash; without negotiating with a landlord, without rent escalation, and without the displacement risk that lease-dependent institutions face. The buildings become permanent assets of the institution's balance sheet, appreciating in value over decades, and providing collateral capacity that supports the institution's long-term financing. The campus plan, in this sense, is also a balance-sheet strategy: the property portfolio is the institutional asset base.</p>

   269→<!-- ─── II. PROPERTY ACQUISITION STRATEGY ─── -->
   270→<div class="chapter-header">
   271→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter II</div>
   272→  <div class="chapter-title">Property acquisition strategy.</div>
   273→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   274→</div>
   275→
   276→<p class="lead">The acquisition of fifty College buildings and three Central Node campuses across thirty-five countries is the largest single line item in the founding campaign &mdash; $82M of the $100M goal, allocated to the Place pillar. The acquisition strategy specifies how buildings are sourced, evaluated, acquired, and integrated into the planetary network.</p>
   277→
   278→<p class="body-text">Building sourcing begins twelve to eighteen months before a College is scheduled to open. The founding team identifies candidate cities based on the tier framework (Tier A, B, or C), then surveys the city's existing building stock for properties that meet four criteria: adequate floor area (typically 8,000&ndash;25,000 square meters, depending on tier); appropriate floorplates for tutorial rooms, lecture halls, library, and commons; central location with transit access; and a building condition that permits renovation within the budget and timeline constraints. Candidate buildings include repurposed convents and monasteries (often vacant due to declining religious orders), warehouses and factories (vacated by industrial relocation), office complexes (underutilized due to remote-work shifts), and former schools or government buildings (decommissioned due to demographic change).</p>
   279→
   280→<p class="body-text">Each candidate building is evaluated against a standard assessment template covering structural condition, environmental performance, regulatory status, acquisition cost, renovation cost, and operating cost. The assessment is conducted by a local architect and a local surveyor, with the founding team providing the institutional specification. Buildings that pass the assessment enter the acquisition pipeline; buildings that fail are recorded for future reference. The pipeline is maintained at three to five candidate buildings per planned College, ensuring that the institution can proceed with an alternative if the preferred building is withdrawn or if the acquisition cost exceeds the budget envelope.</p>

   299→<p class="body-text">Acquisition is structured as a fee-simple purchase wherever local law permits, which is the case in forty-two of the fifty planned College cities and all three Central Node cities. In the eight cities where foreign-institution ownership is restricted, the institution acquires the building through a long-term lease (typically 99 years) with a local foundation partner that holds legal title in trust for the university. In every case, the institution's operating control over the building is absolute; only the legal form of the title varies.</p>

The renovation timeline is six to twelve months for Tier C Colleges, nine to fifteen months for Tier B Colleges, twelve to eighteen months for Tier A Colleges, and fifteen to twenty-four months for Central Nodes &mdash; with the longer timelines reflecting the more extensive specification (convocation halls, founding archives, hybrid-delivery AV infrastructure) at the higher tiers. Renovation is sequenced to permit occupancy in the academic year following acquisition, ensuring that the founding cohort can be admitted on schedule.</p>

Even at 50% naming uptake, the naming gifts cover the property costs: the donor names a College, the College owns the building, and the building is permanent.</p>

   307→
   308→<p class="body-text">The acquisition strategy also includes a portfolio-level reserve. Approximately 8% of the Place pillar ($6.6M) is held as a contingency reserve to cover cost overruns, currency fluctuations, and the acquisition of additional properties if a planned College cannot be opened in its target city. The reserve is governed by the Board of Visitors and reported on the annual audit. The portfolio approach &mdash; rather than a per-College budget &mdash; permits the institution to absorb the variability inherent in fifty simultaneous property acquisitions across thirty-five countries without jeopardizing the launch schedule.</p>

   317→<p class="lead">The three Central Nodes &mdash; Venice, San Francisco, and Singapore &mdash; are the apex of the planetary network. Each Node serves as the regional seat of governance, convocation, and the founding document archive for one third of the planetary university. The Nodes are the structural mechanism by which the institution's governance is distributed across three legally and culturally distinct jurisdictions, preventing capture by any single national government.</p>

   319→<p class="body-text">The three Central Nodes were selected by a single criterion: their capacity to serve as neutral, permanent, geographically distributed seats of the institution.    320→
   321→<p class="body-text">The tri-continental governance structure is the institutional defense against political capture. A university whose governance is concentrated in a single country is vulnerable to changes in that country's regulatory regime, political climate, or diplomatic posture. A university whose governance rotates physically between three jurisdictions &mdash; with the founding document archived in Venice, the technology charter in San Francisco, and the Asia-Pacific convening seat in Singapore &mdash; is structurally protected against any single government's intervention. The Nodes are not only academic campuses; they are the constitutional architecture of the institution's independence.</p>

   433→<p class="body-text">San Francisco was selected as the innovation seat for three reasons. The first is geographic: the San Francisco Bay Area is the global center of the technology economy, with the highest concentration of venture capital, startup activity, and technology talent in the world. Locating the Forge incubator in this ecosystem provides the planetary university's spinout engine with direct access to the capital, talent, and commercialization infrastructure that the technology economy provides. The second is institutional: the Bay Area's existing research universities (Stanford, Berkeley, UCSF) provide a collegial ecosystem of complementary research capacity, with collaboration pathways in biotech, computational science, and adjacent fields.</p>
   434→
   435→<p class="body-text">The third reason is symbolic. The planetary university's commitment to innovation as one of its four academic pillars (alongside epistemology, computational thinking, and global systems) requires a permanent physical presence in the geography that has most defined twenty-first-century innovation. San Francisco is not the only city with a technology economy, but it is the city whose identity has been most shaped by the technology economy &mdash; and the institution that anchors its innovation seat there inherits that identity. The Forge incubator, the computational research programs, and the biotech corridor partnerships are the substantive content of the San Francisco Node; the city's symbolic gravity is the institutional context in which that content is delivered.</p>
   436→
   437→<p class="body-text">The San Francisco Node houses approximately 5,000 students drawn from across the Americas and the Pacific technology arc, with the curriculum delivered in the standard Artemis format. The Node also houses the Forge incubator &mdash; the institution's startup-spinout engine, which takes research from the planetary university's Centers of Inquiry and develops it into commercial ventures, with the institution retaining a 5% equity stake in each spinout. The Forge equity portfolio is one of the institution's five revenue lines at maturity, projected to generate $18M per year in distributions and exits. The Node also houses the computational and biotech research programs &mdash; including the Synthetic Intelligence Center of Inquiry (San Francisco&ndash;Toronto&ndash;Edinburgh), the Biotech and Life Sciences Center (Cambridge UK&ndash;Basel&ndash;Guangzhou), and the Urban Futures Center (Copenhagen&ndash;Medellín&ndash;Singapore).</p>

   447→<div class="chapter-header">

   450→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>

   452→
   453→<p class="lead">Singapore is the Asia-Pacific seat of the planetary university. The Singapore Central Node houses the Asia-Pacific convening hall, the fintech and urban futures research programs, and the institution's outreach to the Indian Ocean basin. The Node occupies a repurposed commercial complex in the city's innovation district, with proximity to the regional headquarters of the global financial and technology firms that anchor Singapore's economy.</p>
   454→
   455→<p class="body-text">Singapore was selected as the Asia-Pacific seat for four reasons. The first is geopolitical neutrality: Singapore is a sovereign city-state whose diplomatic posture is independent of any of the major regional powers (China, India, Japan, Indonesia, Australia), and whose regulatory and legal framework is internationally trusted. A university whose Asia-Pacific seat is in Singapore is therefore convening in a jurisdiction that none of the regional powers can claim as their own &mdash; the structural condition for neutral convening in a region whose geopolitical tensions are otherwise unavoidable. The second is geographic centrality: Singapore sits at the meeting point of the Indian Ocean, the South China Sea, and the Pacific, with direct flight connections to every major city in the Eastern hemisphere.</p>
   456→
   457→<p class="body-text">The third reason is regulatory: Singapore's legal framework for foreign-institution ownership, research conduct, and intellectual property is among the most stable and well-established in the Asia-Pacific region, providing the institutional certainty required for a permanent campus. The fourth reason is research infrastructure: Singapore's existing research ecosystem &mdash; including the National University of Singapore, Nanyang Technological University, the A*STAR research institutes, and the city's financial and urban-planning agencies &mdash; provides a collegial ecosystem of complementary research capacity, particularly in fintech, urban futures, and the digital infrastructure of the smart city.</p>
   458→
   459→<p class="body-text">The Singapore Node houses approximately 5,000 students drawn from across the Asia-Pacific and the Indian Ocean basin, with the curriculum delivered in the standard Artemis format. The Node also houses the Fintech, DeFi, and Economics Center of Inquiry (London&ndash;Singapore&ndash;Lagos), the Urban Futures Center (Copenhagen&ndash;Medellín&ndash;Singapore), and the Bio-Regenerative Arts Center (Venice&ndash;Rotterdam&ndash;Singapore) &mdash; three of the institution's 19 research centers, each distributed across multiple nodes. The Asia-Pacific convening hall hosts the institution's periodic gatherings of students, faculty, and partners from across the Eastern hemisphere, providing the neutral meeting point that the region's diplomatic posture otherwise lacks.</p>

   468→<!-- ─── VIII. THE GLOBAL NODE NETWORK ─── -->
   469→<div class="chapter-header">
   470→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter VIII</div>
   471→  <div class="chapter-title">The global node network.</div>
   472→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   473→</div>
   474→
   475→<p class="lead">The fifty Colleges and three Central Nodes form a single network &mdash; not a federation of independent institutions, but one university operating across fifty-three physical locations. Students move between nodes. Faculty rotate. Knowledge is produced everywhere and shared everywhere. The network architecture is the mechanism by which the planetary university is planetary rather than merely multicampus.</p>
   476→
   477→<p class="body-text">The network operates on a single shared curriculum, a single faculty roster, and a single treasury. Every Artemis College delivers the same nine degree programs, the same four academic pillars, the same tutorial model, and the same graduation requirements &mdash; with local electives that reflect the city's particular intellectual resources (a Berlin College elective in Continental Philosophy, a San Francisco Node elective in Venture Formation, a Nairobi College elective in East African History). The faculty roster is shared: a professor based in Venice may teach a tutorial in Singapore in the fall and a lecture in Berlin in the spring, with the digital infrastructure of the network permitting synchronous and asynchronous delivery across the planetary footprint.</p>
   478→
   479→<p class="body-text">Student mobility is a structural feature of the network. Every Artemis student is entitled to spend at least one academic term at a node other than their home College, with the tuition, credit, and financial aid flowing seamlessly across the network. A student who enters at the Kampala College may spend a term at the Venice Central Node, another at the San Francisco Node, and another at the London College &mdash; accumulating the planetary experience that is the credential's distinctive feature. The mobility program is funded by the operating P&amp;L (not by an additional fee), ensuring that the planetary experience is available to every student regardless of financial circumstance.</p>
   480→
   481→<div class="figure">
   482→  <img src="../public/resources/img/world-night.png" alt="Earth at night from space">
   483→  <div class="caption">The planetary network at night &mdash; fifty Colleges and three Central Nodes across thirty-five countries. The lights are the cities the network is embedded in; the network is the institution that connects them.</div>
   484→</div>
   485→
   486→<p class="body-text">The treasury is single: tuition, endowment payout, research grants, Forge equity distributions, and continuing-education revenue flow into a single institutional treasury, from which faculty compensation, facilities operations, scholarships, research expenditures, and administration are paid. The single-treasury architecture is what permits the operating model to produce the $262M annual surplus from Year 2 onward &mdash; the surplus that funds scholarships, builds the endowment, and underwrites expansion. A federated treasury, with each College managing its own finances, would replicate the cost structure of the existing university system; a single treasury is what permits the planetary university to operate at $5,000 per student per year rather than $90,000.</p>
   487→
   488→<p class="body-text">The network's digital infrastructure is the connective tissue that makes the planetary model possible. Every College is equipped with hybrid-delivery lecture halls (permitting a lecture in Venice to be attended live in Nairobi and São Paulo), tutorial rooms with synchronous collaboration tools, and the asynchronous video library that permits every lecture to be reviewed by every student across the network. The digital infrastructure was, until recently, the missing piece that would have made a planetary university impossible: cloud infrastructure, AI-mediated tutorials, distributed collaboration tools, and translation at scale are now mature enough to support a single shared curriculum across thirty-five countries. The campus plan is, in this sense, also a technology plan: the buildings house the digital infrastructure, and the digital infrastructure houses the network.</p>
   489→
   490→<div class="callout">
   491→  <div class="callout-label">The network's compounding advantage</div>
   492→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">A traditional university adds marginal students at a marginal cost of several thousand dollars per student &mdash; the cost of the physical and administrative capacity required to support them. The Artemis network adds marginal students at a marginal cost approaching zero, because the curriculum, faculty, and digital infrastructure are shared across the network. This is the structural reason the planetary university can offer $3,000 tuition while the Ivy League charges $60,000: the cost per student falls as the network grows, rather than rising as the campus fills.</p>
   493→</div>
   494→
   495→<p class="body-text">The network's expansion path is built into the founding architecture. The fifty Colleges and three Central Nodes are the Year 1 target, but the institution's design horizon extends to a century and beyond. Additional Colleges can be added to the network at marginal cost &mdash; a new College in a city not yet on the map requires acquisition and renovation ($2M&ndash;$25M, depending on tier), connection to the shared digital infrastructure, and integration into the faculty rotation, but does not require additional curriculum development, additional administrative overhead, or additional central infrastructure. The network is therefore not only the founding configuration of the planetary university but the expansion architecture by which the institution grows over the century that follows its founding.</p>
