   246→<!-- ─── I. THE ARCHITECTURE OF NAMING ─── -->
   247→<div class="chapter-header">
   248→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter I</div>
   249→  <div class="chapter-title">The architecture of naming.</div>
   250→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   251→</div>
   252→
   253→<p class="lead">Naming at Artemis is structured around two distinct financial instruments: the property acquisition and the endowment. A College or Central Node naming funds the acquisition of a physical asset that the institution owns in perpetuity. A professorship, center, degree program, or scholarship naming funds an endowment whose yield covers a recurring cost in perpetuity. Understanding the difference is the prerequisite to understanding what each naming tier buys.</p>
   254→
   255→<p class="body-text">A property naming &mdash; Tier C, Tier B, Tier A, or Central Node &mdash; converts the donor's gift into the acquisition of a building. The building is selected from existing stock (a repurposed convent, warehouse, factory, or office complex) and acquired in fee simple. The donor's name attaches to the building, the College housed within it, and every graduate who passes through it for as long as the institution owns the property. Because the property is owned rather than leased, the naming right is coextensive with the asset &mdash; the name endures as long as the building does, which under Artemis's repurposing philosophy means centuries rather than decades.</p>
   256→
   257→<p class="body-text">An endowment naming &mdash; Distinguished Professorship, Center of Inquiry, Degree Program, or Scholarship Fund &mdash; converts the donor's gift into a restricted corpus whose annual yield covers a specific recurring cost. The corpus is invested under the institution's investment policy, governed by the Board of Visitors, and reported on the annual audit. At a 4.5% spending rate, a $5M endowment generates $225,000 per year &mdash; enough to fund one senior faculty position in perpetuity. A $10M endowment generates $450,000 per year &mdash; enough to operate a research center. A $3M endowment generates $135,000 per year &mdash; enough to underwrite a degree program. A $12K scholarship funds one student for four years at $3,000 per year.</p>
   258→
   259→<div class="callout">
   260→  <div class="callout-label">The two naming instruments</div>
   261→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;"><strong>Property naming</strong> (Tiers C/B/A College + Central Node): the gift acquires a building. The donor's name attaches to the building and the College it houses, in perpetuity. The asset is owned; the name is permanent.<br><br><strong>Endowment naming</strong> (Professorship + Center + Degree + Scholarship): the gift establishes a restricted corpus. The corpus yields 4.5% per year in perpetuity. The donor's name attaches to the recurring cost &mdash; a position, a center, a program, a life &mdash; that the yield covers, forever.</p>
   262→</div>
   263→
   264→<p class="body-text">The eight tiers described in this booklet cover every scale of giving, from the $12K scholarship that funds a single student's four-year journey to the $25M Central Node that anchors an entire continent in the planetary network. Every tier is open to every donor. Every tier is structured to close the founding campaign. Every tier is governed by the same accountability architecture &mdash; a binding gift agreement, an annual audit, a permanent seat on the Board of Visitors (for lead namings), and the legal recourse preserved to the donor's estate if the institution deviates from the terms of the gift.</p>

The donor's $5M gift is placed in a restricted corpus invested under the institution's investment policy &mdash; a diversified portfolio targeting a long-term net return of 6-7% with a 4.5% spending rate. The 4.5% spending rate produces $225,000 in the first year, which covers the named professor's compensation, benefits, and research allowance. The remaining 1.5-2.5% of net return is retained in the corpus to offset inflation, ensuring that the $225,000 maintains its real purchasing power across decades. The corpus compounds; the spending rate is held constant; the position is funded forever.</p>
