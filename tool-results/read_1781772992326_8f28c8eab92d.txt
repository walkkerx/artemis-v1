   500→<div class="chapter-header">

   505→
   506→<p class="lead">The repurposing philosophy is the institution's primary climate instrument. New academic construction is among the most carbon-intensive activities in the modern economy, and a planetary university built from scratch would expend the carbon of a small city before admitting a single student. By repurposing fifty-three existing buildings, the institution avoids between 60% and 80% of the embodied carbon that new construction would require, while operating the network at a fraction of the per-student energy footprint of the traditional research university.</p>
   507→
   508→<p class="body-text">The carbon case for repurposing is mathematical. A new academic building of 15,000 square meters &mdash; the typical Tier A College specification &mdash; embodies between 4,500 and 13,500 tonnes of CO2 in its concrete, steel, glass, and finishes. A repurposed building of equivalent floor area, by contrast, typically requires only 1,000 to 3,000 tonnes of CO2 in renovation materials, mechanical systems, and finishes. Across fifty-three campuses, the saving is between 175,000 and 555,000 tonnes of CO2 &mdash; the annual carbon footprint of a city of 30,000 people. The planetary university cannot claim to address the twenty-first century's challenges while expending the twentieth century's construction carbon to build itself.</p>
   509→
   510→<p class="body-text">The operational carbon case is equally compelling. New academic buildings typically consume between 250 and 400 kilowatt-hours per square meter per year, driven by deep floorplates, mechanical ventilation, and intensive cooling loads. Repurposed buildings, with shallower floorplates and natural cross-ventilation possible through original window arrangements, typically consume between 120 and 200 kWh per square meter per year &mdash; a 40% to 50% reduction in operational energy. Across the 600,000-square-meter planetary portfolio, the operational saving is on the order of 60 to 100 gigawatt-hours per year, the consumption of a small city.</p>

The 2,000-faculty roster at maturity is recruited, compensated, and rotated centrally &mdash; with each faculty member holding a home College and rotating through the network for at least one term every three years. A professor based in Venice may teach a tutorial in Singapore in the fall, a lecture in Berlin in the spring, and a research sabbatical in San Francisco in the summer. The rotation ensures that every College, regardless of tier, has access to the institution's full faculty &mdash; the structural condition for the network's claim that a graduate of the Kampala College holds the same degree as a graduate of the London College.</p>
   579→
   580→<p class="body-text">Faculty compensation is set centrally, on a single global salary scale calibrated to the local cost of living in each College city. The single scale prevents the salary arbitrage that would otherwise hollow out Tier C Colleges (where local market rates are low) or inflate Tier A Colleges (where local market rates are high). A professor in Nairobi is paid on the same scale as a professor in New York, adjusted for housing and tax differentials, ensuring that the network's faculty is recruited on the basis of intellectual fit rather than compensation differential. The single scale is also the institution's defense against the brain drain that has historically disadvantaged universities in lower-income countries.</p>
   581→
   582→<div class="figure">
   583→  <img src="../public/resources/img/students-global.png" alt="Students collaborating across the planetary network">
   584→  <div class="caption">The student experience of the planetary network &mdash; every Artemis student is entitled to spend at least one academic term at a node other than their home College, with tuition, credit, and financial aid flowing seamlessly across the network.</div>
   585→</div>
   586→
   587→<p class="body-text">The convocation calendar is the rhythm of the academic year. Each Central Node hosts one annual founding convocation: Venice in October (the founding seat, where new graduates are sworn in), San Francisco in February (the innovation seat, where the Forge portfolio is reviewed), and Singapore in June (the Asia-Pacific seat, where the regional gatherings convene). The three convocations are open to every student and faculty member, with travel funded from the operating P&amp;L for the founding cohort. The convocation calendar is the mechanism by which the planetary network acquires its institutional identity &mdash; the rhythm by which a network of fifty-three campuses becomes a single university.</p>
   588→
   589→<p class="body-text">The single treasury is the financial connective tissue. Tuition, endowment payout, research grants, Forge equity distributions, and continuing-education revenue flow into a single institutional account, from which faculty compensation, facilities operations, scholarships, research, and administration are paid. The single treasury is what permits the operating model to produce the $262M annual surplus from Year 2 onward, and what permits the institution to charge $3,000 tuition while the Ivy League charges $60,000. A federated treasury, with each College managing its own finances, would replicate the cost structure of the existing university system; the single treasury is what makes the planetary model financially distinctive.</p>
   590→
   591→<div class="callout">
   592→  <div class="callout-label">The single salary, single treasury, single curriculum</div>
   593→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">Three architectural decisions make the planetary university possible: a single faculty salary scale (no arbitrage), a single institutional treasury (no federation), and a single shared curriculum (no local variation in degree). Together, these decisions are what convert fifty-three buildings into a single university &mdash; and what distinguish the planetary model from a federation of independent institutions.</p>
   594→</div>
   595→
   596→<p class="body-text">The operational infrastructure is supported by an administrative staff of approximately 200 FTE at maturity &mdash; 3% of the operating budget, against the 15% to 25% administrative overhead typical of traditional research universities. The administrative compression is possible because the network shares its administrative functions: a single admissions office, a single registrar, a single financial-aid office, a single facilities team, a single IT team, a single development office. The administrative overhead is the operational expression of the network's compounding advantage: marginal students and marginal faculty are added at marginal administrative cost, because the shared infrastructure already exists.</p>
   597→
   598→<!-- ─── XII. THE CENTURY AHEAD ─── -->
   599→<div class="chapter-header">
   600→  <div class="chapter-tag">Chapter XII</div>
   601→  <div class="chapter-title">The century ahead &mdash; expansion beyond Year 1.</div>
   602→  <div class="chapter-divider"></div>
   603→</div>
   604→
   605→<p class="lead">The fifty-three campuses of Year 1 are not the final configuration of the planetary university &mdash; they are the founding configuration. The institution's design horizon extends to a century and beyond, with the network architecture permitting expansion at marginal cost as new cities are added, new research centers are endowed, and new generations of students are admitted. The campus plan is therefore also a century-scale expansion plan: the founding architecture is the seed of an institution that grows over the century that follows its founding.</p>
   606→
   607→<p class="body-text">The expansion path is built into the network architecture. A new College in a city not yet on the map requires acquisition and renovation ($2M&ndash;$25M, depending on tier), connection to the shared digital infrastructure, and integration into the faculty rotation &mdash; but does not require additional curriculum development, additional administrative overhead, or additional central infrastructure. The marginal cost of adding the fifty-fourth College is therefore on the order of $5M, against the average cost of $1.6M per College in the founding fifty. The network's compounding advantage is that each new College becomes cheaper to add than the one before, because the shared infrastructure is already in place.</p>
   608→
   609→<p class="body-text">The strategic expansion priorities for the second decade are the cities the founding fifty does not reach: second-tier cities in sub-Saharan Africa (Accra, Addis Ababa, Dakar, Dar es Salaam), the secondary capitals of South Asia (Colombo, Kathmandu), and the emerging knowledge cities of Latin America (Medellín, Curitiba, Monterrey). These cities are the access mission's second wave &mdash; the places where the planetary university's $3,000 tuition and need-blind admission will have the largest marginal impact on the access pipeline. The expansion is sequenced to maintain the founding commitment: governance first, then flagship presence, then regional capital, then access &mdash; with the second decade weighted toward access.</p>
   610→
   611→<p class="body-text">The research infrastructure expands in parallel. The nineteen Centers of Inquiry of Year 1 are the founding configuration of a research portfolio that is expected to grow to forty or fifty centers by Year 25, as new endowments are added and new research domains emerge. Each new Center is endowed at $10M (the Center of Inquiry naming tier), with the endowment generating $450K per year in perpetuity to fund the center's research program. The expansion of the research portfolio is therefore self-funding &mdash; each new center is capitalized by a donor gift, with the operating P&amp;L covering the marginal cost of integration into the existing research infrastructure.</p>
   612→
   613→<div class="figure">
   614→  <img src="../public/resources/img/governance-building.png" alt="Governance architecture of the planetary university">
   615→  <div class="caption">The governance architecture &mdash; the Board of Visitors, the Academic Council, and the Founders' Council are the institutional structures that govern the planetary university across the century ahead, with the founding document archived at Venice as the constitutional anchor.</div>
   616→</div>
   617→
   618→<p class="body-text">The century-scale design horizon also shapes the institution's relationship with its buildings. Each Artemis building is owned, not leased, and is therefore maintained to a century-scale specification: roofs are replaced on a 50-year cycle, mechanical systems on a 25-year cycle, finishes on a 10-year cycle. The maintenance schedule is funded from the operating P&amp;L, with the facilities budget calibrated to the long-term preservation of the asset rather than the short-term minimization of cost. The buildings appreciate in value over the century, providing collateral capacity that supports the institution's long-term financing &mdash; the campus plan as a balance-sheet strategy, sustained across generations.</p>
   619→
   620→<div class="callout">
   621→  <div class="callout-label">The century metric</div>
   622→  <p class="body-text" style="margin:0;">The institution measures its success on century-scale metrics: the number of buildings still owned and operated, the number of degree programs still taught, the number of graduates who have lived their full careers under the institution's charter. The founding cohort will not see the institution's century. The donors who name its buildings will not see it. The campus plan is designed for the generations that will &mdash; the students of 2125, the faculty of 2075, the graduates whose careers the planetary university will anchor across the entire twenty-first century.</p>
   623→</div>
   624→
   625→<p class="body-text">The campus plan closes with the institutional commitment that motivates the century-scale design horizon. The planetary university is founded not as a venture with an exit, but as a permanent institution whose architecture, finances, and operations are designed to outlive every founder, every donor, and every century in which it operates. The fifty-three campuses of Year 1 are the seed; the century ahead is the institution's actual horizon. The campus plan is the document by which that horizon is made operational &mdash; repurposed, not built; owned, not leased; permanent.</p>
